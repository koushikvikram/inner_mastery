# Study Guide: The 15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership

This study guide provides a comprehensive review of the foundational principles and the first six commitments of conscious leadership as outlined in the provided text. It is designed to facilitate a deep understanding of the shift from unconscious to conscious leadership.

## Part II: Answer Key

1.  **Above the line vs. Below the line:** Leading from above the line is characterized by being open, curious, and committed to learning. Conversely, leading from below the line is defined by being closed, defensive, and committed to being right, often driven by a perceived threat to one's ego or identity.
2.  **Unconscious leadership:** Unconscious leaders are reactive, often operating on "autopilot" driven by past stories or imagined futures. They lack responsiveness to environmental stimuli and remain trapped in habitual patterns of personality, fear, and defensive reactivity.
3.  **Victim consciousness:** In the "To Me" state, leaders see themselves as "at the effect of" external circumstances, blaming people, the economy, or conditions for their state. This mindset is characterized by "why" questions and the pursuit of validation for being right while others are wrong.
4.  **Radical responsibility as a gateway:** Radical responsibility involves choosing to locate the cause and control of one’s life internally rather than blaming external factors. By taking 100% responsibility for their circumstances, leaders shift from being a victim of their environment to being a conscious creator of their experience.
5.  **Figuring it out vs. Wonder:** "Figuring it out" is a fear-based energy that assumes there is a single right answer to be found through mental effort and competition. "Wonder" is an open-ended, child-like state of curiosity that steps into the unknown and uses "wonder questions" to explore possibilities without an attachment to being right.
6.  **Triple benefit of emotional intelligence:** Practicing Commitment 3 allows a leader to experience emotions fully, release them completely (typically in under ninety seconds), and learn from the specific wisdom each emotion offers. This process prevents emotions from hardening into long-term moods like bitterness or anxiety.
7.  **Withholding and energy:** Withholding is the act of refraining from revealing facts, thoughts, feelings, or sensations to relevant parties. This behavior dampens or blocks the flow of "life force" or energy, leading to individual depletion and organizational lethargy or boredom.
8.  **Fact vs. Story:** A fact is objective, unarguable data that a video camera could record, such as specific words spoken or a person arriving at a certain time. A story is an interpretation, judgment, or assumption made about those facts, which—while valuable—is arguable and not inherently true.
9.  **Criteria for gossip:** A statement is considered gossip if it is made about another person with negative intent. It is also gossip if the speaker is unwilling to share the statement in exactly the same way if the person being discussed were in the room.
10. **Redefining integrity:** The text defines integrity as "wholeness," specifically involving the unbroken flow of energy and life force. It also includes "congruence," where a leader’s internal experience (feelings and thoughts) matches their external expression.

## Part IV: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Above the Line** | A state of being open, curious, and committed to learning; the hallmark of conscious leadership. |
| **Below the Line** | A state of being closed, defensive, and committed to being right; a reactive state driven by the ego's survival instinct. |
| **To Me** | A state of consciousness where one feels "at the effect of" life; characterized by victimhood and blaming external causes. |
| **By Me** | A state of consciousness where one sees themselves as the cause of their experience; characterized by curiosity and radical responsibility. |
| **Through Me** | A state of consciousness where one notices something beyond the self (e.g., the quantum field or spirit) and allows life to manifest through them via surrender. |
| **As Me** | A state of "oneness" or non-duality where there is no separation between the leader and the world, and no personal "me" to have a problem. |
| **Radical Responsibility** | The commitment to take full responsibility for one's circumstances and well-being, locating the locus of control inside oneself. |
| **Drama Triangle** | A fear-based interaction involving the roles of Victim (complaining), Villain (blaming), and Hero (over-functioning to temporarily relieve tension). |
| **Shifting** | The master skill of moving from a closed/defensive state (below the line) to an open/curious state (above the line). |
| **Drift** | The natural occurrence of losing presence and falling into reactivity; the "Drift/Shift" model notes that while everyone drifts, conscious leaders shift quickly. |
| **Emotional Literacy** | The ability to define emotion as "energy in motion" and identify the five core sensations: anger, fear, sadness, joy, and sexual feelings. |
| **Unarguable Communication** | Speaking only of one's own thoughts, feelings, and sensations (e.g., "I'm having the thought that...") which others cannot logically dispute. |
| **Withholding** | Refraining from revealing facts, thoughts, feelings, or sensations, which leads to relational disconnection and energy depletion. |
| **Clearing Model** | A structured communication tool used to end gossip and resolve issues by separating facts from stories and expressing desires. |
| **Integrity** | Wholeness; characterized by the uninterrupted flow of energy, keeping agreements, and congruence between internal experience and external expression. |
| **Wonder Questions** | Open-ended questions (e.g., "I wonder what I can learn here?") that have no "right" answer and invite high learning states. |
| **Congruence** | The matching of what is on the inside (experience) with what is expressed on the outside. |
| **Projection** | A defense mechanism where one attributes their own hidden feelings, behaviors, or motivations to others (e.g., "You spot it, you got it"). |
