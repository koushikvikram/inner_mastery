# Study Guide: A Century Is Not Enough by Sourav Ganguly

This study guide provides a comprehensive review of the themes, events, and leadership philosophies outlined in Sourav Ganguly’s memoir. It is designed to facilitate a deep understanding of the professional and personal challenges faced by one of India’s most successful cricket captains.

## Part 2: Quiz Answer Key

1.  The 1992 tour was a period of "wilderness" where Ganguly felt like a "net bowler" rather than a batsman, scoring only 3 runs in his debut. The experience taught him the harsh reality of international cricket, highlighting the need for mental toughness and technical adjustments to handle additional pace and bounce.
2.  Haynes taught Ganguly that seven out of ten successes are born from positive visualization, whereas failures often stem from surrendering mentally. He emphasized that winners handle pressure by viewing it as an opportunity and calculating their success in their minds the night before a game.
3.  Scoring 131 on debut at Lord's placed Ganguly’s name on the hallowed Honours Board, which he viewed as a sign of permanent arrival in international cricket. The performance earned him the respect of veteran teammates, notably symbolized by Sachin Tendulkar helping him fix his bat tape during the innings.
4.  Ganguly aimed to change the team's culture by instilling a "killer instinct" and a fearless attitude, particularly in overseas conditions. He focused on identifying and backing young talent and shifted the strategy to include more fast bowlers to win matches outside the subcontinent.
5.  Ganguly traveled to Australia in July 2003, months before the team, to train with Greg Chappell and perform a personal "recce" of the grounds. He used this time to measure slip-cordon distances and adjust to the cold, damp conditions, ensuring he would not be surprised by the environment during the actual tour.
6.  "Bapi Bari Ja" is a Kolkata slang term used when a batsman hits a ball into the stands, literally meaning "Bapi, go home." For Ganguly, it became a personal war cry and a message to his critics that he was capable of bouncing back and demolishing their arguments through his performance.
7.  The relationship turned "turbulent" when Chappell allegedly poisoned the atmosphere, eventually writing a secret email to the BCCI claiming Ganguly was mentally and physically unfit to lead. Chappell argued that the team was "fearful and distrusting" of Ganguly, leading to his unceremonious removal from the captaincy and the squad.
8.  During the Sydney Test, Andrew Symonds accused Harbhajan Singh of racial abuse, leading to a massive international crisis where the Indian team threatened to pull out of the series. The incident unified the team under Anil Kumble’s leadership, eventually leading to a historic victory in the subsequent Test at Perth.
9.  Following the advice of Sunil Gavaskar, Ganguly used visualization to prepare for the marathon innings required on bouncy South African tracks. He mentally rehearsed negotiating hostile pace, telling himself he might "get hit or break a bone" but would not take a backward step, which resulted in a crucial 51 not out at Wanderers.
10. Ganguly announced his retirement during Durga Puja because he felt he had reached "The End" emotionally and wanted to go out on his own terms. Although he was still performing well, he was tired of being on "indefinite trial" by selectors and wanted to walk away with his head held high.

## Part 4: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Bapi Bari Ja** | A Bengali phrase meaning "Bapi, go home"; used in cricket to describe hitting a ball for a six, symbolizing dominance over the bowler. |
| **Bisharjon** | The immersion of the deity Durga in the river Ganga, marking the end of Durga Puja; used by Ganguly as a metaphor for the end of his career. |
| **Board President’s XI** | A secondary Indian team used to vet youngsters or assess the form of veterans whose Test future is uncertain. |
| **Dhak/Dhunuchi** | Traditional elements of Durga Puja; the *dhak* is a drum and *dhunuchi* refers to a traditional dance performed during evening arati. |
| **Gaddafi Stadium** | A prominent cricket ground in Lahore, Pakistan, where the Indian team played several historic matches. |
| **Lord’s Honours Board** | A prestigious board in the Lord's pavilion that records the names of players who have scored a century or taken five wickets in a Test match at the ground. |
| **Monkeygate** | The 2008 controversy involving Andrew Symonds and Harbhajan Singh, centered on allegations of racial abuse during the Sydney Test. |
| **NatWest Series** | A one-day international tournament in England (2002) famous for India’s comeback victory and Ganguly waving his shirt on the Lord's balcony. |
| **Prince of Calcutta** | A nickname bestowed upon Ganguly by Geoffrey Boycott to acknowledge his graceful batting style and leadership. |
| **The Gabba** | A cricket ground in Brisbane, Australia, known for its extra bounce and pace, where Ganguly made his international debut in 1992. |
| **Wanderers** | A stadium in Johannesburg, South Africa, where Ganguly led India to a historic Test win and made a significant personal comeback in 2006. |
| **WACA** | The Western Australian Cricket Association ground in Perth, renowned for being one of the quickest and hardest pitches in the world. |
