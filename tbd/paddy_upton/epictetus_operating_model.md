# Epictetus Operating Model — Paddy Upton


![Epictetus Operating Model — Paddy Upton](./epictetus_operating_model.png)

> Source frame: Upton's **barefoot coaching** rejects the guru-coach who has all the answers; the coach's job is to make the athlete **their own best coach** by handing back responsibility for learning. Performance is built through a **Learning Cycle — Plan → Reflect → Analyse → Practice** — run honestly and repeatedly, so improvement comes from self-awareness rather than instruction. The enemy of execution is the **distracted mind**, which wanders to the **past** (overconfidence or low confidence) or the **future** (premeditation or fear of failure); the only place to perform is **now — focus only when the ball is in play**. Mental conditioning is trainable: **meditation trains the brain to return to the present**, and reflective practice turns experience into skill. The athlete who owns their learning, regulates arousal, and stays present beats the one who waits to be told what to do.

## Layer 1 — Practice (What must be done?)

### What we’re trying to achieve
- Make yourself your own best coach — own your learning and decisions.
- Stay present when it counts; switch on only when "the ball is in play."
- Turn every session and match into data via honest reflection.
- Train the mind to return from past/future wandering to the now.

### Non-negotiable behaviors
- **Take responsibility**: don't outsource your development to the coach — drive it.
- **Run the Learning Cycle**: Plan → Reflect → Analyse → Practice, every cycle.
- **Switch on/off deliberately**: full focus when the ball is live, genuine rest between.
- **Catch the wandering mind**: notice drift to past or future and return to now.
- **Reflect honestly**: no ego-protection in the debrief — see what actually happened.

### Routines / checklists

- **Pre-session plan (2–5 min)**
  - Define the one or two things you're working on today.
  - State the intention: what does good look like in this session?
  - Decide your focus cue for "ball in play."

- **The Learning Cycle (every session/match)**
  - **Plan**: set the specific intention before you start.
  - **Reflect**: immediately after, recall what happened without judgment.
  - **Analyse**: separate what worked, what didn't, and why.
  - **Practice**: feed the lesson into the next rep/session.

- **Present-moment switch (in competition)**
  - Between balls/points: relax, breathe, switch *off*.
  - As the ball comes into play: switch fully *on* — one point of focus.
  - If the mind goes to the score, the last error, or the result — name it and return.

- **Distracted-mind reset (when focus drifts)**
  - Notice: am I in the past (over/under-confident) or future (premeditating/fearing)?
  - Drop the story; bring attention to a present anchor (breath, target, ball).
  - Re-enter the now before the next action.

- **Meditation block (daily, 10 min)**
  - Sit; rest attention on the breath.
  - Each time the mind wanders, gently return — this *is* the training.
  - Treat returns as reps for in-competition refocusing.

- **Honest reflection journal (post-performance)**
  - What did I intend? What actually happened?
  - What will I do differently next time — specifically?
  - Where did ego stop me from seeing clearly?

### What good execution looks like
- You can coach yourself through a problem without waiting for instruction.
- Focus is sharp when the ball is live and genuinely released between plays.
- Debriefs are honest and produce one concrete adjustment.
- You catch mind-wandering early and return to the present quickly.
- Confidence rests on preparation and presence, not on the last result.

## Layer 2 — Principles (Why does it work?)

- **The athlete must own the learning**: lasting improvement comes from self-awareness, not dependence on the coach.
- **The present is the only place you can perform**: past and future are where focus (and energy) leak away.
- **Reflection converts experience into skill**: unexamined reps don't compound; analysed ones do.
- **Attention is trainable**: meditation strengthens the muscle of returning to now.
- **Switching off enables switching on**: sustained focus is impossible without deliberate recovery.
- **The coach is a guide, not a guru**: answers found by the athlete stick; answers given fade.

### Decision rules (doctrine)
- If you're waiting to be told what to do, take responsibility and decide.
- If your mind is on the score or a past error, switch off and reset to the present.
- If a session ends, run the cycle — reflect and analyse before moving on.
- If confidence is wobbling, check it against preparation, not against the last result.
- If you can't focus continuously, train the switch (off between, on when live).
- If reflection is flattering you, you're protecting ego, not learning.

## Layer 3 — Meta (How do we evaluate and improve?)

### Metrics (leading, self-coaching-tied)
- **Learning-cycle completions** (sessions run fully through Plan→Reflect→Analyse→Practice).
- **Present-moment refocus count** (drifts caught and reset during competition).
- **Self-coaching incidents** (problems solved without waiting for the coach).
- **Meditation consistency** (days the focus-training block was done).
- **Honest-reflection rate** (debriefs that produced a real, specific adjustment).

### Review loop
- **Daily**: did I switch on/off deliberately and catch my wandering mind?
- **Weekly**: which learning cycles produced a concrete change? Where did I dodge honest reflection?
- **Monthly**: am I more my own coach now — making and owning decisions — than before?

### Core rule guardrail
- If you're performing while mentally in the past or future, you aren't performing — you're spectating your own thoughts. Switch off, reset to the present, and switch on only when the ball is in play. And if you've started waiting for the coach to fix you, stop: pick up the Learning Cycle and coach yourself.

---

## Operating Sequence

1. Plan the session intention and your "ball in play" focus cue.
2. Switch on fully when live; switch off and recover between.
3. Catch mind-wandering to past/future and return to the present.
4. Reflect and analyse honestly; extract one concrete adjustment.
5. Practice the lesson — and own the whole cycle as your own coach.
