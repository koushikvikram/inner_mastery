# Epictetus Operating Model — Sports Performance Psychology

> Source frame: elite performance is governed less by talent than by how the mind **appraises and regulates** pressure. The first lever is **stress appraisal** — the same physiological alarm read as a **challenge** ("go forward") rather than a **threat** ("run away") changes the entire response; **reappraisal** is the trainable shift from threat to challenge (Sam Martin; cognitive reappraisal). The second is **emotional granularity** — naming emotions precisely (journaling, an emotional vocabulary) instead of suppressing them, because suppression degrades performance. The third is **attention control** — focus is the hidden ingredient of excellence (Goleman's inner/other/outer focus; Dandapani's "awareness is a ball of light," concentration = keeping it on one thing); most people unwittingly *practice distraction*. The fourth is **anti-avoidance** — playing the long game, embracing the boring, not waiting to feel perfect, and not playing it safe (Beswick: fighter vs. victim; "What do you want? How badly? How much will you suffer?"). Underneath sits **arousal regulation** — meditation and breath to quiet Self 1 (the critical, overthinking mind) and trust Self 2 (the natural body; Gallwey's *Inner Game*).

## Layer 1 — Practice (What must be done?)

### What we’re trying to achieve
- Read pressure as a challenge to engage, not a threat to flee.
- Name and process emotions precisely instead of suppressing them.
- Train attention so focus, not distraction, is the practiced default.
- Stop avoidance behaviors: play the long game, embrace the boring, act before feeling perfect.
- Regulate arousal so the natural, trained self (Self 2) performs without interference.

### Non-negotiable behaviors
- **Reappraise on cue**: when the body fires up, label it "challenge / I'm ready," not "danger."
- **Watch your language**: self-coach in supportive, forward language, not catastrophizing.
- **Name the emotion**: use a precise word, not "I feel bad" — granularity over suppression.
- **Anchor attention**: keep awareness on one chosen object; return it when it drifts.
- **Refuse avoidance**: don't wait to feel perfect, don't play it too safe, don't skip the boring work.
- **Quiet Self 1**: stop over-instructing yourself mid-performance; trust the trained body.

### Routines / checklists

- **Pre-performance reappraisal (2–3 min)**
  - Notice the arousal (heart rate, nerves) and name it as readiness.
  - Reframe: "This pressure means it matters — challenge, not threat."
  - Set forward-looking self-talk: what I *will* do, not what I fear.

- **Emotional vocabulary journal (4-prompt technique)**
  - What am I feeling? (precise word)
  - Where/why is it coming from?
  - What is it telling me?
  - What do I want to do with it? (process, don't suppress)

- **Attention training (daily meditation, 10 min)**
  - Rest awareness on one anchor (breath).
  - Each drift, return — this is the rep that builds concentration.
  - Remember: you get good at what you practice; practice focus, not distraction.

- **Focus-direction check (in competition)**
  - Inner focus: am I calm and clear inside?
  - Other focus: am I reading opponents/teammates?
  - Outer focus: am I tracking the field/conditions?
  - Park whichever is leaking; lock onto the one that matters now.

- **Anti-avoidance audit (weekly)**
  - Did I wait to "feel ready" instead of acting?
  - Did I play it safe to avoid failure?
  - Did I skip the boring fundamentals?
  - Did I obsess without recovery? Schedule the fix.

- **Self 1 / Self 2 reset (when overthinking)**
  - Notice the critical, instructing voice (Self 1).
  - Drop the verbal commentary; give the body a simple target.
  - Trust the trained instinct (Self 2) to execute.

- **Beswick three questions (commitment check)**
  - What do you want?
  - How badly do you want it?
  - How much are you willing to suffer for it?

### What good execution looks like
- Pressure spikes are read as fuel, not danger.
- Emotions are named and used, not bottled up or vented blindly.
- Focus holds on the right target and returns quickly when it drifts.
- You do the boring, fundamental work and act before feeling perfect.
- In the moment, the mind is quiet and the body performs on training.

## Layer 2 — Principles (Why does it work?)

- **Appraisal determines response**: challenge vs. threat changes physiology, cognition, and outcome from the same starting arousal.
- **Reappraisal is trainable**: the threat→challenge shift is a skill, not a personality trait.
- **Granularity beats suppression**: precisely named emotions can be regulated; suppressed ones leak into performance.
- **Attention is the hidden ingredient of excellence**: you become good at whatever you practice — most practice distraction.
- **Avoidance is the silent killer**: waiting to feel perfect, playing safe, and skipping the boring stops 99% of athletes.
- **Self 2 outperforms Self 1**: peak performance comes from quieting the overthinking mind and trusting the natural body.
- **Fighter, not victim**: the long game and willingness to suffer separate those who dominate from those who merely turn up.

### Decision rules (doctrine)
- If arousal spikes, label it challenge/readiness before doing anything else.
- If your self-talk turns catastrophic, switch to forward, supportive language.
- If you feel "bad," name the precise emotion before reacting.
- If attention drifts, return it to one chosen anchor — don't chase the distraction.
- If you're waiting to feel perfect, act now anyway.
- If you're overthinking mid-performance, quiet Self 1 and trust Self 2.
- If you're playing safe to dodge failure, reframe and commit to the challenge.

## Layer 3 — Meta (How do we evaluate and improve?)

### Metrics (leading, regulation-tied)
- **Reappraisal rate** (pressure moments read as challenge vs. threat).
- **Emotional-granularity entries** (precise emotion namings / week).
- **Attention-return reps** (focus drifts caught and reset, in training and competition).
- **Anti-avoidance count** (instances of acting before "feeling ready," doing the boring work, not playing safe).
- **Self 1 quieting** (performances run on instinct vs. over-instruction).
- **Recovery adherence** (rest taken to balance obsession/training).

### Review loop
- **Daily**: did I reappraise pressure, name emotions, and train attention?
- **Weekly**: where did avoidance creep in (safe play, waiting to feel perfect, skipped fundamentals)? Where did Self 1 take over?
- **Monthly**: am I appraising more as challenge, focusing more reliably, and recovering enough to sustain it?

### Core rule guardrail
- If you're suppressing emotion, fleeing pressure as a threat, or waiting to feel perfect, you've handed the game to the 99%. Name the feeling, reappraise the alarm as a challenge, anchor your attention, and act — then quiet the critical mind and let the trained body perform.

---

## Operating Sequence

1. Reappraise the arousal: challenge, not threat.
2. Name the emotion precisely; process rather than suppress.
3. Anchor attention on the one thing that matters now.
4. Refuse avoidance — act before feeling perfect, do the boring work.
5. Quiet Self 1, trust Self 2, then recover to sustain the long game.
