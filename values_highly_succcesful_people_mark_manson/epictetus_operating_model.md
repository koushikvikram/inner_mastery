# Epictetus Operating Model — Mark Manson, *Values & the One Principle Highly Successful People Share*


![Epictetus Operating Model — Mark Manson, *Values & the One Principle Highly Successful People Share*](./epictetus_operating_model.png)

> Source frame: you are always "giving a [__]" — attention is hardwired, so the real question is *what* you choose to prioritize. The quality of your life is the quality of your **problems**; happiness is not the absence of struggle but the presence of meaningful struggle ("which struggles am I happy to have?"). Good values pass three filters: **controllable**, **reality-based**, and **socially constructive** — people-pleasing fails the first, illusions fail the second, ego-in-disguise fails the third. Motivation is the *effect* of action, not the cause: the **"Do Something" principle** and **minimum viable action** beat waiting for inspiration. Extraordinary success demands a **contrarian take that is correct, executed with conviction** — being a pariah before a peer. And if you don't want the **process**, you don't want the **result**; **Manson's Law of Avoidance** says you'll dodge whatever threatens your identity, while **memento mori** and a **time audit** cut through the **tyranny of choice**.

## Layer 1 — Practice (What must be done?)

### What we’re trying to achieve
- Narrow focus to a handful of values that matter, ending the bleed of caring about everything.
- Choose problems worth having instead of chasing a struggle-free life.
- Make action the trigger for motivation, not the reward for it.
- Commit hard enough that optionality's noise shuts off.

### Non-negotiable behaviors
- **Audit values against three filters**: controllable, reality-based, socially constructive.
- **Say a conscious yes**: exit unwanted focus by choosing something better, not by "not caring."
- **Do the minimum viable action**: when stuck, take the smallest possible step right now.
- **Want the process, not just the result**: accept the 99% grunt work or admit you're fantasizing.
- **Check Manson's Law**: when you're avoiding something, ask what identity it threatens.
- **Commit to kill the optionality noise**: pick a path and accept the nineteen unlived lives.

### Routines / checklists

- **Three-filter value audit (per value or decision)**
  - **Controllable?** Is this about my behavior, or someone else's reaction? (Honesty > being liked.)
  - **Reality-based?** Is it grounded in fact, or a "should" fantasy that will keep me pissed off?
  - **Socially constructive?** Does it actually help, or is it ego dressed as good intention?

- **"Do Something" loop (when paralyzed)**
  - Rewrite the problem; find the first step (Mr. Pacwood's rule).
  - Do the smallest thing — one sentence, walk into the gym, say "hi."
  - Let the action generate inspiration; the next step reveals itself only once you're in motion.

- **Process-vs-result test (before pursuing a goal)**
  - Name the daily process the result actually requires (the solitary 99%).
  - Ask: "Would I do the boring, ridiculed, misunderstood work even without the trophy?"
  - If no, you want the fantasy, not the career — choose a different problem.

- **"What if I'm wrong?" journaling (when defending identity)**
  - What if I'm wrong? (Consider the perspectives I've ignored.)
  - What would it mean if I'm wrong? (Find the sensitivity I'm protecting.)
  - Would being wrong give me a *better problem* than being right?

- **Memento mori + time audit (clarity reset)**
  - Track hours honestly for five days — behavior reveals real values.
  - Compare actual hours to stated values; cut the discrepancy.
  - Ask "what matters on my deathbed?" to strip trivial anxieties.

### What good execution looks like
- You can name your handful of values and they pass all three filters.
- Stuck moments end with a minimum viable action, not more waiting.
- You're willing to be a pariah for a contrarian bet you've reasoned through.
- You hold identity loosely and treat rejection as a useful sorting mechanism.
- Your calendar matches your stated values; commitment has quieted the noise.

## Layer 2 — Principles (Why does it work?)

- **You can't not care**: every "no" to the trivial is a "yes" to something significant.
- **Quality of life = quality of problems**: you trade one set of struggles for a better set, never for none.
- **Bad values are uncontrollable, unreal, or self-serving**: they guarantee suffering.
- **Action precedes motivation**: inspiration is the effect of doing, not its prerequisite.
- **Wanting the result without the process is fantasy**: the cost *is* the want.
- **Outlier success is contrarian + correct + conviction**: you must be a pariah before a peer.
- **Manson's Law of Avoidance**: you dodge anything — good or bad — that threatens your certainty of who you are.
- **Optionality is tyranny**: commitment is liberation because it removes the noise of unlived lives.

### Decision rules (doctrine)
- If a value depends on others' reactions, downgrade it — keep the controllable version.
- If you're attached to how the world "should" be, drop the illusion before it pisses you off.
- If you're stuck, do the smallest possible thing instead of seeking motivation.
- If you only want the result, not the process, don't start — pick a better problem.
- If you're avoiding something, ask which identity it threatens, then move toward it.
- If optionality is paralyzing you, commit — the screens go dark the moment you choose.

## Layer 3 — Meta (How do we evaluate and improve?)

### Metrics (leading, value-first)
- **Filter pass rate** (share of your active values that are controllable, reality-based, socially constructive).
- **Time-audit alignment** (hours spent vs. stated values — the honest scoreboard).
- **Minimum-viable-action count** (times you broke paralysis by doing the smallest thing).
- **Process adherence** (showing up for the boring 99%, not just craving the 1%).
- **Avoidance flags** (identity-threatening things dodged this week).
- **Problem quality** (are this week's struggles ones you're happy to have?).

### Review loop
- **Daily**: one minimum viable action taken; one "do something" win.
- **Weekly**: time audit vs. values — where's the discrepancy I'm pretending isn't there?
- **Monthly**: process check — am I doing the work the result requires, or fantasizing about the title?
- **Quarterly**: memento mori review — would my deathbed self endorse how I'm spending my [__]?

### Core rule guardrail
- If you're optimizing audits, journals, and routines but still avoiding the one identity-threatening action that matters, the meta has become a sophisticated form of avoidance. Stop analyzing, do the smallest real thing, and choose the struggle worth having — the result takes care of itself.

---

## Operating Sequence

1. Audit your values against the three filters; keep the few that pass.
2. When stuck, do the minimum viable action — let motivation follow.
3. Confirm you want the process, not just the result.
4. Make the contrarian bet (correct + conviction) and accept pariah status.
5. Run the time audit and memento mori; commit, kill the noise, repeat.
