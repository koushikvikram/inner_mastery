# Why Your Search for Security is Killing You: 5 Radical Lessons on Courage

We are a culture of ostriches. We bury our heads in the sand of "five-year plans," insurance policies, and the desperate hope that tomorrow will look exactly like today. We call this safety. Osho calls it a "beautiful imprisonment." 

The hard truth is that **only idiots feel secure.** To be truly alive is to be in constant contact with the unpredictable mystery of existence. If the future were certain, life would be nothing more than a dead, mechanical process—a bogus script already written. 

Even God, if such a thing exists, cannot know what will happen next. If He did, he would be a mere technician overseeing a factory of robots. Life is grandeur precisely because it is an unknown freedom. To seek certainty is to commit spiritual suicide. 

## Lesson 1: Stop Calling it Insecurity—Call it Freedom

The mind is a creature of habit. It views the open gate of the unknown and recoils in terror, much like a prisoner who has spent decades in a cell. To the prisoner, the "safety" of the jail—the guaranteed meals, the fixed routine—is preferable to the "insecurity" of the outside world.

We do the same. We cling to our misery because it is familiar. We fear the unknown because it requires us to be alert, to be aware, and to take responsibility.

> "Don’t call it uncertainty—call it wonder. Don’t call it insecurity—call it freedom."

When you stop seeking formulas, you become intelligent. An intelligent person does not rely on a map; they rely on their own light. They don't know what is going to happen, and they are thrilled by that very fact.

## Lesson 2: The Heart is a Gambler; The Head is a Businessman

To live with courage, you must recognize the internal civil war between your head and your heart. Your head is a businessman. It is cunning, calculating, and composed entirely of the "known"—your accumulated past and borrowed memories. It can only "chew the already chewed."

The heart, however, is a gambler. It is non-calculating and lives in the gateway of the future. While the head builds walls and calls them logic, the heart seeks the open sea.

> "The heart is always ready to take the risk, the heart is a gambler. The head is a businessman. The head always calculates—it is cunning."

Meaning is not found in the safety of the head; it is discovered through the risks of the heart. A life lived purely through the head is already in the grave. To be vitally alive, you must follow the heart's whispering, even when it leads you into the "uncomfortable" or the "arduous."

## Lesson 3: Courage is Vulnerability, Not Bravery

We often mistake bravery for courage. In Osho’s view, **brave people are merely cowards turned upside down.** They have created an armor, a defense mechanism to hide their fear. They are not fearless; they are merely well-defended.

True courage is not the absence of fear, but the ability to act in spite of it. It is the willingness to be vulnerable, to sail an uncharted sea like Columbus without the protection of a map.

Consider the story of the sannyasin Dandamis. When Alexander the Great threatened to behead him for refusing to leave India, Dandamis laughed. He told the world-conqueror: "You can only cut my body, and that I left long ago. I am a witness." 

Alexander was forced to sheath his sword. He realized that **you cannot enslave a man who is ready to die.** It is your fear that makes you a slave; when you are vulnerable and ready for the unknown, you are beyond the reach of any tyrant.

## Lesson 4: Burn the Map: Life is a Dynamic Ganges

Rules and commandments are outdated the moment they are written. Life is not a stagnant pool; it is a "dynamic Ganges" that changes every second. To live by a code is to be spineless and unintelligent. 

Most people are "carbon copies," repeating the "chewed" answers of the past. This is why they are taken unawares by the spontaneity of existence. 

Recall the Zen story of the two boys from rival temples. One boy, trained in "philosophical" answers, claimed he was going "wherever the wind takes me." When challenged on what he would do if the wind stopped, he was paralyzed. His rival, living afresh in the moment, simply said: "I am going to fetch vegetables from the market." 

The first boy was a prisoner of logic; the second was a master of the moment.

> "Commit as many mistakes as possible, remembering only one thing: don’t commit the same mistake again."

## Lesson 5: Love is an Expansion; Fear is a Shrinkage

Fear is not a positive force; it is the absence of love, just as darkness is the absence of light. When you are in fear, you shrink, you close, and you lose contact with existence. 

Society and families hinder love because **a person in love cannot be manipulated.** They cannot be sent to war to kill strangers, because a heart full of love is creative, not destructive. To move from fear to the "vast purity" of existence, one must move through the four stages of love:

1.  **Sex:** Finding "at-homeness" in the body and dispelling physical fear.
2.  **Love:** Unconditional connection that cleanses the mind of its bargaining.
3.  **Prayer:** A spontaneous, heart-to-heart dialogue with the universe. Reject the "authorized" versions. Like the three simple saints who prayed, "You are three, we are three, have mercy on us," your prayer must be your own.
4.  **Meditation:** The final stage where even fearlessness disappears, leaving only a virgin silence.

> "Fear is nothing but absence of love."

## The Responsibility of Joy

We are the sole architects of our misery or our bliss. To be miserable is a cowardly habit; it requires no effort and allows you to blame the world, your parents, or your fate. 

To be blissful is the greatest act of courage. It requires you to divorce your oldest companion—suffering—and accept total responsibility for your state of being. 

If death were to knock today, would you be found clinging to the corpse of your past, or would you be standing in the wonder of the unknown? The choice to jump into the light of your own integrity is yours, and it is available this very moment.
