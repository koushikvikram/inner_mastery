## Part 2: Answer Key

1.  **How does the text define a "fixed mindset"?**
    A fixed mindset is the belief that your basic qualities, such as intelligence or personality, are carved in stone and cannot be changed. This belief creates an urgency for individuals to prove themselves over and over, as they view every situation as a test of their inherent traits.

2.  **How does the text define a "growth mindset"?**
    A growth mindset is based on the belief that your basic qualities can be cultivated through effort, strategies, and help from others. Individuals with this mindset believe that while people differ in initial talents and aptitudes, everyone can change and grow through application and experience.

3.  **What did Alfred Binet, the inventor of the IQ test, believe regarding human intelligence?**
    Contrary to the modern use of IQ tests to measure unchangeable intelligence, Binet believed that education and practice could bring about fundamental changes in intelligence. He argued that with training and method, individuals could increase their attention, memory, and judgment to become more intelligent than they were before.

4.  **According to the brain-wave study at Columbia, what specific information captures the attention of people with a fixed mindset?**
    People with a fixed mindset only show interest when the feedback reflects on their ability, such as being told whether their answers were right or wrong. They show no interest in information that could help them learn, even after they have answered a question incorrectly.

5.  **What is "CEO Disease" as described in the text?**
    "CEO Disease" refers to a leader's desire to be seen as perfect and to reign from atop a pedestal. Leaders with this condition, like Lee Iacocca, often surround themselves with worshipers, exile critics, and choose short-term strategies to look like heroes rather than pursuing long-term organizational growth.

6.  **How do the two mindsets differ in their view of "effort"?**
    In a fixed mindset, effort is seen as a sign of deficiency; if you were truly smart or talented, you wouldn't need to work hard. In a growth mindset, effort is what "ignites" ability and turns it into accomplishment, and it is considered the primary tool for developing one's potential.

7.  **What was the primary finding of the study regarding the effects of praising a student's ability versus their effort?**
    The study found that praising ability pushed students into a fixed mindset, making them reject challenges and lose confidence when faced with difficulty. Conversely, praising effort encouraged a growth mindset, with 90 percent of those students choosing more challenging tasks and showing improved performance over time.

8.  **How does the growth mindset impact an individual's experience with depression?**
    While both mindsets experience distress, growth-minded individuals react to depression with determination and action, keeping up with their lives and responsibilities. In contrast, those with a fixed mindset tend to ruminate on failures as a sign of unworthiness, leading them to let their responsibilities slide as they become more despondent.

9.  **Describe the "low-effort syndrome" often seen in adolescents with a fixed mindset.**
    Adolescents with a fixed mindset may view school as a place where they are being judged, leading them to stop trying as a way to protect their egos. By exerting as little effort as possible, they can claim that any failure was due to a lack of trying rather than a lack of inherent ability.

10. **What does the example of the "Wizard of Menlo Park," Thomas Edison, reveal about the reality of genius and invention?**
    Edison's success was not the result of a single "Eureka" moment by a reclusive loner, but rather the product of a network of time-consuming inventions involving thirty assistants and corporate funding. His true genius lay in his mindset—his curiosity, drive, and constant self-education—rather than an effortless, god-given gift.

## Part 4: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Fixed Mindset** | The belief that human qualities (intelligence, personality, character) are static, unalterable traits that must be constantly validated. |
| **Growth Mindset** | The belief that basic qualities are things an individual can cultivate through effort, specific strategies, and input from others. |
| **CEO Disease** | A leadership failure characterized by the need for constant validation, the rejection of critics, and the avoidance of personal shortcomings to maintain an image of perfection. |
| **Character** | In a sports context, the ability to dig down and find strength even when things are going against you; the mark of a champion who can win even when not playing their best. |
| **Low-Effort Syndrome** | A protective strategy used by fixed-mindset individuals (often adolescents) where they intentionally withhold effort to avoid being measured and found lacking. |
| **Potential** | Defined in the growth mindset as an individual's unknown (and unknowable) capacity to develop skills with years of passion, toil, and training. |
| **The "Natural"** | A person who is believed to achieve success or display talent effortlessly, without the need for practice or training. |
| **Stereotype Threat** | The psychological phenomenon where the evocation of a negative stereotype fills a person's mind with distracting thoughts, lowering their performance (most prevalent in those with a fixed mindset). |
| **Neuroplasticity** | Though the term itself is not used, the text refers to this concept as the capacity for "lifelong learning and brain development" through the formation of new connections when meeting challenges. |
| **Phrenology/Craniology** | Historical, discredited theories mentioned in the text that claimed physical differences (like skull shape) created permanent, unalterable differences in human ability. |
