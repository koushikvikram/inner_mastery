# Metalearning: First draw a map

## Metalearning is Learning about Learning

- Not learning about the object of your inquiry itself, but learning about how knowledge is structured and acquired within this subject.

## The power of your metalearning map

The heart of ultralearning success is being able to see

- how a subject works
- what kinds of skills and information must be mastered
- what methods are available to do so more effectively

Metalearning forms the map, showing you how to get to your destination without getting lost.

## Metalearning over the short term

- Ultralearning, due to its intensity and self-directed nature, has the opportunity for a lot higher variance than normal schooling efforts do.
- This can lead to choosing the wrong skills and material to learn, which is dangerous.
- Metalearning research avoids this problem and helps you seek out points where you might even be able to get a significant advantage over the status quo.

## Metalearning over the long term

- The more ultralearning projects you do, the larger your set of metalearning skills will be.
    - You'll
        - know
            - what your capacity is for learning
            - how to best schedule your time and manage your motivation
        - have
            - well-tested strategies for dealing with common problems
        - gain confidence
            - allows you to enjoy the process of learning more with less frustration

## Why Ultralearning (Understanding your motivation to learn)

Types

- Instrumental (to achieve an objective)
    - If instrumental, first determine whether learning the skill or topic in question will actually help you achieve your goal.
        - Use the "Expert Interview" method to determine
- Intrinsic (for your own sake)

## What you need for Ultralearning (Knowledge and activities you'll need to acquire to be successful)

Divide "what" into

- **Concepts**
    - Ideas that need to be **understood in flexible ways**.
- **Facts**
    - Anything that needs to be **memorized**
    - Sufficient if you remember them as long as you can recall them in the right situations.
- **Procedures**
    - **Actions** that need to be **performed** and **may not involve much conscious thinking**.
    - They need to be **practiced**.

### Using this analysis to draw your map

After brainstorming is done, **underline the concepts, facts and procedures** that are going to be the **most challenging**.

- Gives an idea of the major bottlenecks.
- Start searching for methods and resources to overcome those difficulties.
- Look at particular features of the concetps, facts and procedures you're trying to learn to master them more effectively.

## How to Ultralearn (Resources, environments, methods you'll use when learning)

Methods

## How much planning should you do?

There will always be some uncertainity in your approach, so it's important to **find the sweet spot between insufficient research and analysis paralysis.**

*You know when you're procrastinating, so just get started.*

Methods

- The 10% rule
- Diminishing Returns and Marginal Benefit Calculation

### When and how to do research?

Compare the marginal benefits of metalearning to regular learning.

- Spend a few hours doing more research, then spend a few hours doing more learning.
- Then, do a quick assessment of the relative value of each activity.
- Go ahead with whatever activity gave you the highest return.
- This depends on the "Law of Diminishing Returns". i.e. the more time your invest in an activity, the weaker and weaker the benefits will be.

## Appendix

**The Expert Interview Method**

- Talk to people who have already achieved what you want to achieve.
- Particularly valuable for vetting instrumental projects.
- Place to find experts
    - Workplaces
    - Conferences
    - Seminars
    - Social Networking Websites
    - Online Forums
- Most experts are more than willing to offer advice and are flattered by the thought that someone wants to learn from their experience.
- Steps
    - Send a concise and non-threatening email.
    - Request < 15 minutes of their time.
    - Don't request ongoing mentorship.
- It's better to ask for an in-person meeting or phone call rather than text.
- Express interest and enthusiasm in your work and the conversation.

**Benchmarking**

- If a skill is taught in school or university
    - look at the curricula on their website for learning that subject.
        - Eg. syllabus for single course, syllabus for a degree. Consider university websites like MIT, Harvard, Stanford, Yale, etc.
- If the skill is professional or non-academic
    - search online for people who've previously learned that skill or use the Expert Interview method to focus on resources available for mastering that subject.

**Emphasize/Exclude Method**

Steps

1. Find areas of study that align with the goals you've identified in the first part of your research.
2. Omit or delay elements of your benchmarked curriculum that don't align with your goals.

**The 10% rule**

- Rule of thumb
    - Invest about 10% of your total expected learning time into research prior to starting.
    - If project scope is larger, invest about 5% of your time in research.
- **The goal is not to exhaust every learning possibility, but simply to make sure you haven't latched onto the first possible resource/method without thinking through alternatives.**
- Before starting, be aware of the following along with their strengths and weaknesses
    - common methods of learning
    - popular resources
    - tools

**Diminishing Returns and Marginal Benefit Calculation**

- Metalearning isn't a one-time activity.
- Continue to do research as you learn more.
- Obstacles and opportunities aren't clear before you start, so re-assessing is a necessary step of the learning process.
