# Retrieval: Test to Learn

Our idol for retrieval: Srinivasa Ramanujan

> "It pays better to wait and recollect by an effort from within, than to look at the book again." - William James, psychologist
> 

## The Best Strategy for Preparing for an Exam

3 choices you have for allocating your limited studying time for preparing for an exam

1. **Review the material**
    - Look over your notes and book and study everything until you're sure you'll remember it.
2. **Test yourself (free recall)**
    - Keep the book shut and try to remember what was in it.
    - Includes feedback.
    - Outperforms the other choices.
3. **Create a concept map**
    - Write out the main concepts in a diagram, showing how they're organized and related to other items you need to study.

*The act of trying to summon up knowledge from memory is a powerful learning tool on its own, beyond its connection to direct practice or feedback.*

When Ramanujan figured out theorems from "A Synopsis of Elementary Results in Pure and Applied Mathematics" by himself, he was forced to invent his own solutions to the problems, retrieving information from his mind rather than reviewing it in a book.

Ramanujan's genius was aided by 2 hallmarks of the Ultralearner's toolkit

- Obsessive Intensity
- Retrieval Practice

## If retrieval practice is so much better for learning than the other choices, why don't students realize it?

Possible explanations

- Humans don't have the ability to know with certainty how well they've learned something.
    - Our Judgements of Learning (JOL) are based, in part, on how fluently we can process something.
        - If the learning task feels easy and smooth, we are more likely to believe we've learned it.
        - If the task feels like a struggle, we'll feel we haven't learned it yet.
        - Minutes after studying something using a strategy of passive review, students performed better than if they would if they practiced retrieval.
        - However, when tested again days later, retrieval practice beats passive review by a mile.
        - *What helped in the immediate time after studying turns out not to create the long-term memory needed for actual learning to take place.*
- Students don't feel they know the material well enough to test themselves on it.
    - Students who were performing weakly elected to review the material first, waiting until they were "ready" to start practice testing.
    - If through experimental intervention, however, they were forced to practice retrieval earlier, they learned more.

*Whether you're ready or not, retrieval practice works better. Especially, if you **combine retrieval practice with the ability to look up the answers**, retrieval practice is a much better form of studying than the ones most students apply.*

## Is Difficulty Desirable?

- More difficult retrieval leads to better learning, provided the act of retrieval is itself successful
- Free recall tests > Cued recall tests > Recognition tests
- Giving someone a test after a slight delay, long enough that answers aren't in mind when they need them improves retention better than testing immediately after they learn something
- Difficulty may be part of the reason retrieval works
- Pushing difficulty higher and opting to test oneself well before they feel "ready" is far more efficient
- *Difficulty can become undersirable if it gets so hard that retrieval becomes impossible. If you delay the test too long, the information may be forgotten. So, find the right midpoint.*

## Should you take the final exam before the class even begins?

- Regular testing of previously studied information can make it easier to learn new information.
- Retrieval works to enhance future learning even when there is noting to retrieve yet.
- Practicing retrieval might not only benefit from starting earlier than one is "ready" but even before you have the possibility of answering correctly.

## What Should be Retrieved?

Determine through **Direct Practice + Passive Exposure**

- Direct Practice forces you to retrieve the things that come up often in the course of using the skill.
- However, relying on Direct Practice alone means the knowledge that isn't in your head can't help you solve problems.
    - Direct Practice alone can fail to encourage enough retrieval by omitting knowledge that can help you solve a problem but isn't strictly necessary to do so.
        - Eg. If a programmer has 2 ways to solve his problem, and Option A is more effective, but Option B will also get the job done. Suppose he knows only about Option B and later reads about Option A on Stackoverflow. Simply reading alone won't help. He'll forget about it when it comes to apply the technique. **Great programmers often know dozens of ways to solve problems and can select the best one for each situation.**
- Breadth requires passive exposure, which in turn benefits from retrieval practice.

## Tactics to Practice Retrieval

1. Flash Cards
2. Free Recall
3. The Question-Book Method
4. Self-Generated Challenges
5. Closed-Book Learning

## Appendix

**Free recall tests**

- Recall as much as you can without prompting

**Cued recall tests**

- Given hints about what you need to remember

**Recongition Tests**

- Correct answer needs to be recognized, not generated.
    - Eg. Multiple Choice Questions

**Flash Cards**

- Simple, yet effective way to learn **paired associations between questions and answers**.
- Spaced-repetition systems are a modern way of using flash cards.
- Works really well for
    - Memorizing Foreign-Language Vocabulary
    - Maps
    - Anatomical Diagrams
    - Definitions
    - Equations
- Drawback
    - Doesn't work well when the situation in which you need to remember the information is highly variable.
        - Eg. Programmers can memorize syntax via flash cards, but concepts that need to be applied in real programs often don't fit the cue-response framework that flash cards demand.

**Free Recall**

- After reading a section from a book or sitting through a lecture, try to write down everything you can remember on a blank piece of paper.
- Difficult as you'll miss many things, however, that's a reason for this practice being helpful.
    - By forcing yourself to recall the main points and arguments, you'll be able to remember them better.

**The Question-Book Method**

- Rephrase your notes as questions to be answered later.
    - Thereby, you generate material to practice retrieval later on.
- Lazily restating the factual content in the book as questions isn't very useful.
- Restate the **big ideas of a chapter or section as a question.**
    - Requires deeper thinking.
- Restrict yourself to 1 question per section so that you focus on the main point.
- Works well for retrieval of simple information.

**Self-Generated Challenges**

- If you're trying to practice a skill, such as being able to code an algorithm, **create challenges for yourself to solve later**.

**Closed-Book Learning**

- Nearly any activity can become an opportunity for retrieval if you cut off the ability to search for hints.
    - Eg. On a test that relies on creating a concept map, students who did closed-book concept mapping during preparation did better than those that prepared by creating a concept map by looking at a book.
- Any practice, whether direct or a drill, can be cut off from the ability to look things up.
    - Thereby, the information becomes knowledge stored inside your head instead of inside a reference manual.
