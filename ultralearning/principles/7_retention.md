# Retention: Don't Fill a Leaky Bucket

> "Memory is the residue of thought" - Daniel Willingham, cognitive psychologist
> 

Memory is essential to learning well, even when it is wrapped up in bigger ideas such as understanding, intuition, or practical skill.

Being able to understand how something works or how to perform a particular technique is useless if you cannot recall it.

## Why is it so Hard to Remember Things?

Our minds are a leaky bucket; however, most of the holes are near the top, so the water that remains at the bottom leaks out more slowly.

- Decay
    - Forgetting with Time
- Interference
    - Overwriting Old Memories with New Ones
- Forgotten Cues
    - A Locked Box with No Key

## How can you Prevent Forgetting?

Forgetting is the default, not the exception.

Memory Mechanisms

1. Spacing
    - Repeat to Remember
2. Proceduralization
    - Automatic Will Endure
3. Overlearning
    - Practice Beyond Perfect
4. Mnemonics
    - A Picture Retains a Thousand Words

These methods roughly tackle 2 similar but different problems.

1. Retention
    - Particularly important when the volume of information to be learned is often so large that forgetting becomes a practical obstacle almost immediately.
2. Longevity of the skills and knowledge acquired after the project has been completed

## Appendix

**Interference: Overwriting Old Memories with New Ones**

- Our memories overlap one another in how they are stored in the brain. Therefore, memories that are similar but distinct can compete with one another.
- Important Flavors
    - Proactive interference
    - Retroactive interference

**Proactive interference**

- Occurs when previously learned information makes acquiring new knowledge harder.
    - Think of this as if the “space” where that information wants to be stored is already occupied, so forming the new memory becomes harder.
- Can happen when you want to learn the definition of a word but have difficulty because that word already has a different association in your mind.

**Retroactive interference**

- Learning something new “erases” or suppresses an old memory.

**Forgotten Cues: A Locked Box with No Key**

- Many memories we have aren’t actually forgotten but simply inaccessible. If you say you remember something, you should be able to retrieve it from memory.
- Since we aren’t constantly experiencing the entirety of our long-term memories simultaneously, this means there must be some process for dredging up the information, given an appropriate cue.
- If one of the links in the chain of retrieving the information has been severed (perhaps by decay or interference), the entire memory may become inaccessible.
    - However, if that cue were restored, or if an alternative path to the information could be found, we would remember much more than is currently accessible to us.
- Advantages of this explanation:
    - Intuitively, this feels true as we know the tip-of-the-tongue experience, when we feel as though we should be able to remember a fact or word but we’re not able to summon it up immediately.
    - Suggests that relearning things is much faster than learning them initially, because relearning is closer to repair work, while original learning is a completely new construction.
    - Forgetting cues seems highly likely as a partial explanation of forgetting many things.
    - “Lost” memories that are retrieved through new cues may actually be fabrications.

**Spacing: Repeat to Remember**

- If you care about long-term retention, don't cram.
- Spreading learning sessions over more intervals over longer periods of time tends to cause somewhat lower performance in the short run (because there is a chance for forgetting between intervals) but much better performance in the long run.
    - Eg. Instead of doing one class at a time, do a few in parallel, to minimize the impact that the crammed study time would have on your memory.
- If you have 10 hours to learn something, spend 10 days studying 1 hour each than spend 10 hours studying in one burst.
- However, if the amount of time between study intervals gets longer and longer, the short-term effects start to outweigh the long-term ones.
- Use the spaced-repetition-system (SRS) for retaining the most knowledge with the least effort.
    - However, SRS tends to have quite focused applications.
    - It's more difficult to apply to more complicated domains of knowledge, which rely on complex information associations that are built up only through real-world practice.
- Semi-regular practice of a skill is often quite helpful.
- When it comes to retention, don't let perfect become the enemy of good enough.
- Semi-regularly do refresher projects.
    - Disadvantage: sometimes deviates quite a lot from optimal spacing
        - However, if you’re prepared to do a little bit of relearning to compensate, it can still be a better approach than completely giving up practice.
        - Scheduling this kind of maintenance in advance can also be helpful, as it will remind you that **learning isn’t something done once and then ignored but a process that continues for your entire life**.

**Proceduralization: Automatic Will Endure**

- Procedural skills, such as riding a bicycle, are stored in a different way from declarative knowledge, such as knowing the Pythagorean Theorem.
    - This difference may also have different implications for long-term memory.
- Procedural skills are much less susceptible to being forgotten than knowledge that requires explicit recall to retrieve.
- Using this difference to our advantage: Most skills proceed through stages - starting declarative, but ending up procedural as you practice more.
- This process may fail. However, if you practice more and more, it'll work out. Eventually, you may start to remember it by feel.
- Instead of learning a large volume of knowledge or skills evenly, emphasize a core set of information much more frequently, so that it becomes procedural and is stored far longer.
- Most skills we learn are incompletely proceduralized.
- Some skills cannot be completely automated and will always require some conscious thought.
- Strategies for applying Proceduralization
    1. Ensure that a certain amount of knowledge is completely proceduralized before practice concludes.
    2. Spend extra effort to proceduralize some skills, which will serve as cues or access points for other knowledge.
        - You may aim to completely proceduralize the process you use to start working on a new programming project, for example, so that you can get over that hump in the process of writing a new program.

**Overlearning: Practice Beyond Perfect**

- Additional practice, beyond what is required to perform adequately, can increase the length of time that memories are stored.
- Learning
    - The time from zero to the point where we've practiced a task enough to do it correctly once.
- Overlearning
    - Practice that continues after the first correct application. Performance doesn't improve, but the durability of the skill gets extended.
- Overlearning is typically a short-term phenomenon, but when **combined with spacing and proceduralization**, it has longer-term implications.
- Dovetails nicely with the principle of directness.
    - Direct use of a skill frequently involves overpracticing certain core abilities.
        - So, that kernel is usually quite resistant to forgetting, even years later.
    - Academically learned subjects, in contrast, tend to distribute practice more evenly to cover the entire curriculum to a minimum level of competency in each area.
- 2 main methods for applying overlearning
    1. **Core Practice**: Continually practicing and refining the core elements of a skill.
        - Works well paired with some kind of **immersion or working on extensive projects**, after the initial ultralearning phase has been completed.
    2. **Advanced Practice**: Going one level above a certain set of skills so that core parts of the lower-level skills are overlearned as one applies them in a more difficult domain. Eg. Studying Calculus (a more advanced skill) enables retaining Algebra (a lower-level skill required for studying Calculus)

**Mnemonics: A Picture Retains a Thousand Words**

- Commonalities of Mnemonic Strategies
    1. Designed to remember very specific patterns of information.
    2. Usually involve translating abstract or arbitrary information into vivid pictures or spatial maps.
- *Mnemonics can be learned and applied by anyone patient enough to apply them.*
    - Eg. Method for learning foreign languages: **Keyword Method**
        - Take a foreign-language word and convert it into something that sounds like in your native language.
        - Next, create a mental image that combines the sounds-like version of the foreign word and an image of its translation in a fantastical and vivid setting that is bizzare and hard to forget.
- Recommended reading: Moonwalking with Einstein: The Art and Science of Remembering Everything
- Applications of Mnemonics are very narrow and in many real-world settings, they simply aren't worth the hassle.
- Disadvantages
    1. Most Mnemonic Systems require considerable upfront investment.
    2. Recalling from Mnemonics isn't as automatic as directly remembering something.
- Mnemonics can act as a bridge for difficult-to-remember information, but it's usually not the final step in creating memories that will endure forever.
- If you are doing a task that requires memorizing highly dense information in a very specific format, especially if the information is going to be used over a few weeks or months, they can enable you to do things with your mind that you might not have thought possible.
- Alternatively, they can be used as an intermediate strategy to smooth initial information acquisition when the information is quite dense.
- In the modern world, which has developed excellent coping mechanisms for the fact that most people cannot remember things as a computer can, mnemonics tend to serve more as cool tricks than as a foundation you should base your learning efforts on.
