# Focus: Sharpen Your Knife

Our Idol for Focus: Mary Somerville

> "I learnt by habit to leave a suject and resume it again at once, like putting a mark into a book I might be reading." - Mary Somerville
> 

Mary Somerville's environment had

- Constant distractions
- Little social support
- Continuous Obligations

How can one, in an enviroment such as her's, manage to focus long enough not only to learn an impressive breadth of subjects, but to such depth?

The struggles people have with focus come in 3 broad varieties

1. Starting
2. Sustaining
3. Optimizing the quality of one's focus

## Problem 1: Failing to start focusing (aka procrastinating)

3 reasons we procrastinate

- There's a **craving** that drives you to do something else.
- There's an **aversion** to doing the task itself.
- Both the above reasons.

Steps to overcome procrastination

1. Recognize when you procrastinate.
    - Make a mental habit of every time you procrastinate.
    - Try to recognize that you're feeling some desire not to do that task or a stronger desire to do something else.
    - This **awareness** is necessary for progress to be made.
2. Think in terms of a **series of "crutches" or mental tools**.
    - Helps you get through your tendency to procrastinate.
    - Series of crutches: 5-minute focus -> Pomodoro technique -> Take a break after you've met a certain condition in your task -> Time-boxing
    - Switching to a harder goal when you're still mostly impeded by an earlier problem may backfire.
    - Go back and forth between the crutches as convenient.

## Problem 2: Failing to sustain focus (aka getting distracted)

**Flow** isn't the ideal kind of focus for ultralearning.

Ultralearning is a form of deliberate practice.

- Requires you to consistently adjust your approach through self-consciousness.

Don't worry about flow.

- In some learning tasks, you'll achieve it easily.
- At the same time, don't feel guilty if flow doesn't come automatically.

Your goal is to enchance your learning.

- Involves pushing through some sessions that are more frustrating than what could be considered ideal for flow.

Remember, even if your learning is intense, your use of skill later on will not be.

*Investments made in pushing through learning now will make skillful practice a much more enjoyable activity down the road.*

How long should you focus?

- Ever-longer periods of focus are not optimal from a learning standpoint.
- People retain more of what they learn when practice is broken into different study periods than when it is crammed together.
- **Interleaving**
    - Even within a solid block of focus, it can make sense to alternate between different aspects of the skill or knowledge to be remembered.
    - Therefore, if you have several hours to study, cover a few topics rather than focus exclusively on one.
    - Trade-off
        - If your study time becomes more and more fractured, it may be difficult to learn at all.
    - Need balance
        - 50 minutes to 1 hour is a good length of time for many learning tasks.
    - Find out what works best for you for retaining the material, based on your
        - schedule
        - personality
        - workflow

3 sources of distraction

1. Environment
2. Task
3. Mind

**Environment**

- Remove notifications and distractions.
- Multitasking is unsuitable for ultralearning.
- Concentrate your full mind on the task at hand.

**Task**

- When you have a choice between using different tools for learning, consider using the one that makes it easier to focus.
- This shouldn't supersede other considerations such as directness and feedback.
- More intense strategies are harder to do in the background of your mind. So, there are fewer opportunites for distractions to creep in.
    - Solving problems
    - Making something
    - Writing
    - Explaining ideas aloud

**Mind**

- Things we need to deal with to prevent ourselves from getting distracted.
    - Negative emotions
    - Restlessness
    - Daydreaming
- 2 sides to these problems
    - A clear, calm mind is best for focusing on almost all learning problems.
        - Don't ignore life issues. Deal with them.
    - Sometimes there's nothing you can do about your emotions, and feelings arise spontaneously without requiring you to do something about them.
        - Acknowledge the feeling, be aware of it, and gently adjust your focus back to your task and allow the feeling to pass.
        - *When the emotion is intense, by not reacting to the emotion at the level of abandoning your task entirely, you'll diminish it's intensity in the future. You'll strengthen your commitment to continue working in future situations like this, so they'll become easier.*

## Problem 3: Failing to create the right kind of focus (optimizing the quality of one's focus)

What's the optimal degree of alertness to maximize your learning?

2 variables we need to keep in mind for applying attention

1. **Arousal** (overall feeeling of energy/alertness)
    - ^ arousal -> ^ alterness, )( focus
        - Good for
            - relatively simple tasks
            - tasks that require intense concentration towards a small target
    - ^^ arousal -> focus starts to suffer
        - You get distracted easily.
    - lower arousal -> ( ) focus - large and more diffuse
        - Good for
            - more complex tasks
            - tasks requiring you to consider many different inputs or ideas
                - Eg. writing essays, solving math problems
    - Arousal can be controlled/modified by changing your environment.
        - Eg. noise increases arousal, which is good when you're ill-rested, but bad when you're well-rested.
2. **Task complexity**

Long focus, followed by no focus is useful when you want to get unstuck while working on a creative task.

Find out what works best for you through self-testing.

## Improving your ability to focus

Focus can be improved through practice to eventually reach Mary Somerville's level of focus.

Discipline in one area doesn't translate to disciple in another area, but the procedure you follow to get better at focusing translates to other areas.

Recognize where you are and start small.

## Appendix

**Procrastination**

- Instead of doing the thing you're supposed to do, you work on something else or slack off.
- 2 forms
    - General: A constant state of life, running away from one task to another until deadlines force you to focus and then having to struggle to get the job done on time.
    - Specific: More acute form that manifests itself with particular kinds of tasks.

**5-minute focus**

- Recognize that most of what is unpleasant in a task or what is pleasant about an alternative task is an impulse that doesn't actually last that long.
- If you actually **start working or ignore a potent distractor**, it usually only takes a **couple of minutes** until the worry starts to dissolve, even for fairly unpleasant tasks.
- Tell yourself that you need to spend only five minutes on the task before you can stop and do something else.
    - This is often enough for you to get started.
    - However, once you start, you may end up continuing for longer without wanting to take the break.

**Pomodoro Technique**

- "25 minutes of focus followed by a 5-minute break"
- Using the 5-minute rule too often isn't very productive.
- In such cases, use the Pomodoro technique.

**Time-boxing**

- If your project is not troubled by extreme procrastination, switch to using a calendar on which you carve out specific hours of your day in advance to work on the project.
- Allows you to make best use of your limited time, but works only if you follow it.

**Flow**

- The state of mind you associate with being "in the zone".
- Enjoyable state that slides right between boredom and frustration, when a task is neither too hard nor too easy.
- Has characteristics that are inconsistent with the demands of deliberate practice.
