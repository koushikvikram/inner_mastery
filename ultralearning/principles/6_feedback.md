# Feedback: Don't Dodge the Punches

> "Everybody has a plan until they get punched in the mouth." - Mike Tyson
> 

## The Power of Information

What separates ultralearning from more conventional approaches is that feedback provided is more

- Immediate
- Accurate
- Intense

The deep dive into feedback is uncomfortable, but the rapid immersion also desensitizes you to a lot of anxiety.

No feedback, and the result is often stagnation and declining abilities.

## Can Feedback Backfire?

More feedback isn't always better. What matters is the type of feedback given.

Feedback works well when it provides useful information that can guide future learning.

- If feedback tells you what you’re doing wrong or how to fix it, it can be a potent tool.

Feedback often backfires when it is aimed at a person’s ego.

- Praise is usually harmful to further learning.
- When feedback steers into evaluations of you as an individual (e.g., “You’re so smart!” or “You’re lazy”), it usually has a negative impact on learning.

Feedback that includes useful information needs to be correctly processed as a motivator and tool for learning.

When seeking feedback, the ultralearner needs to be on guard for two possibilities.

1. Overreacting to feedback (both positive and negative) that doesn’t offer specific information that leads to improvement.
    - Be sensitive to what feedback is actually useful and tune out the rest.
2. Not only can overly negative feedback lower your motivation, but so can overly positive feedback.
    - Balance both concerns, pushing for the right level of feedback for your current stage of learning.

Who is giving the feedback can matter.

- Feedback from a peer or teacher has important social dynamics beyond mere information on how to improve one’s abilities.

Fear of feedback often feels more uncomfortable than experiencing the feedback itself.

- The fear of hearing criticism causes us to shut down.
- Sometimes the best action is just to dive straight into the hardest environment, since even if the feedback is very negative initially, it can reduce your fears of getting started on a project and allow you to adjust later if it proves too harsh to be helpful.

All of these acts require

- self-confidence
- resolve
- persistence

*Ultralearners acquire skills quickly because they seek aggressive feedback when others opt for practice that includes weaker forms of feedback or no feedback at all.*

## What kind of Feedback do you Need?

The opportunities for seeking better feedback will vary depending on what you’re trying to learn.

By knowing what kind of feedback you’re getting, you can make sure to use it best, while also recognizing its limitations.

1. Outcome Feedback
    - Are you doing it wrong?
2. Informational Feedback
    - What are you doing wrong?
3. Corrective Feedback
    - How can you fix what you're doing wrong?

Be careful when trying to “upgrade” feedback from a weaker form to a stronger form if it’s not actually possible. - To switch from outcome feedback to informational feedback, you need to be able to elicit feedback on a per element basis of what you’re doing. - If instead the feedback is being provided as a holistic assessment of everything you’re doing, trying to turn it into informational feedback can backfire, such as leading to guesses from those giving feedback.

Corrective feedback requires a “correct” answer or the response of a recognized expert. - If there is no expert or a single correct approach, trying to turn informational feedback into corrective feedback can work against you when the wrong change is suggested as an improvement.

Ultralearning isn’t simply about maximizing feedback but also knowing when to selectively ignore elements of it to extract the useful information.

## How Quick should Feedback be?

Immediate feedback is superior in settings outside of the laboratory.

Delaying the presentation of the correct response along with the original task (delayed feedback) is more effective. - Presenting the question and answer again offers a second, spaced exposure to the information. - Immediate feedback is best paired with delayed review (or further testing) to strengthen your memory compared with a single exposure.

Don't get feedback before you’ve tried your best to answer the question or solve the problem at hand.

## How to Improve your Feedback

Tactics

1. Noise Cancellation
2. Hitting the Difficulty Sweet Spot
3. Metafeedback
4. High-Intensity, Rapid Feedback

## Beyond Feedback

If you process it as a message about your ego rather than your skills, it’s easy to let a punch become a knockout.

Though short-term feedback can be stressful, once you get into the habit of receiving it, it becomes easier to process without overreacting emotionally. Use this to your advantage, exposing yourself to massive amounts of feedback so that the noise can be stripped away from the signal. Feedback and the information it provides is useful only if you remember the lessons it teaches.

## Appendix

**Outcome Feedback: Are you doing it wrong?**

- Most common and easiest to get.
- Only type of feedback available in many situations.
- Tells you something about how well you’re doing overall but offers no ideas as to what you’re doing better or worse.
- Can come in the form of a grade or in the form of an aggregate feedback to many decisions you’re making simultaneously.
- Can improve how you learn through a few different mechanisms.
    1. Provides you with a motivational benchmark against your goal.
    2. Can show you the relative merits of different methods you’re trying.
        - When you are progressing rapidly, you can stick to those learning methods and approaches.
        - When progress stalls, you can see what you might be able to change in your current approach.

**Informational Feedback: What are you doing wrong?**

- Fairly common.
- Recognize
    - When you can split apart outcomes to get feedback on parts of what you're learning.
    - When feedback only on holistic outcomes is possible.
- Tells you what you’re doing wrong, but it doesn’t necessarily tell you how to fix it.
- Easy to obtain when you can get real-time access to a feedback source.
    - A computer programmer who gets error messages when her programs don’t compile properly may not have enough knowledge to understand what she’s doing wrong.
    - But as errors increase or diminish, depending on what she does, she can use that signal to fix her problems.
    - Self-provided feedback is also ubiquitous, and in some pursuits it can be almost as good as feedback from others.
- Often pairs well with directness because it often comes from direct interaction with the environment.

**Corrective Feedback: How can you fix what you're doing wrong?**

- Toughest to find.
- When employed well, can accelerate learning the most.
- Shows you not only what you’re doing wrong but how to fix it.
- Often available only through a coach, mentor, or teacher.
- Sometimes can be provided automatically if you're using the right study materials.
- Can be unreliable.
    - Situation in which paying for a tutor can be useful, because that person can spot the exact nature of your mistake and correct it with less struggle on your part.

**Noise Cancellation**

- Anytime you receive feedback, there are going to be both a
    - signal
        - the useful information you want to process
    - noise
        - caused by random factors, which you shouldn’t overreact to when trying to improve
        - A real problem when trying to improve your craft because you need to do far more work to get the same information about how to write well.
        - By modifying and selecting the streams of feedback you pay attention to, you can reduce the noise and get more of the signal.
- Look for proxy signals. Eliminates some of the noisy data.
    - Eg. For blog writing, one way to do so would be to use tracking code to figure out what percentage of people read your articles all the way to the end.
    - This doesn’t prove your writing is good, but it’s a lot less noisy than raw traffic data.

**Hitting the Difficulty Sweet Spot**

- Good feedback is very hard to predict and thus gives more information each time you receive it.
- Ultralearners carefully adjust their environment so that they’re not able to predict whether they’ll succeed or fail.
    - If they fail too often, they simplify the problem so they can start noticing when they’re doing things right.
    - If they fail too little, they’ll make the task harder or their standards stricter so that they can distinguish the success of different approaches.
- Try to avoid situations that always make you feel good (or bad) about your performance.

**Metafeedback**

- This kind of feedback isn’t about your performance but about evaluating the overall success of the strategy you’re using to learn.
- One important type of metafeedback is your **learning rate**.
    - Gives you information about how fast you’re learning, or at least how fast you’re improving in one aspect of your skill.
- 2 ways you can use this tool
    1. Decide when you should focus on the strategy you’re already using and when you should experiment with other methods.
        - If your learning rate is slowing to a trickle, that might mean you’re hitting diminishing returns with your current approach and could benefit from different kinds of drills, difficulties, or environments.
    2. Compare two different study methods to see which works better.

**High-Intensity, Rapid Feedback**

- Sometimes the easiest way to improve feedback is simply to get a lot more of it a lot more often.
    - Particularly true when the default mode of learning involves little or infrequent feedback.
- Emotional advantages
    - You may initially feel uncomfortable, but you’ll get over that initial aversion much faster than if you wait months or years before getting feedback.
- Provokes you to engage in learning more aggressively than you might otherwise.
    - Knowing that your work will be evaluated is an incredible motivator to do your best.
    - This motivation may end up outweighing the informational advantage it provides.
