# Step 2: Schedule Your Time

Your ultralearning project doesn’t need to be an intensive, full-time endeavor to succeed.

However, it will require some time investment, and **it’s better to decide on how much time you are willing to devote to learning in advance than simply hope that you’ll find the time later**.

Reasons for planning your schedule ahead of time

1. This way you subconsciously **prioritize** your project by setting it down on your calendar **ahead of other things**.
2. Learning is often frustrating. If you don’t set aside time to learn, it will be a lot harder to **summon up the motivation** to do so.

Decisions you should make

- **How much time you’re going to commit.**
    1. This is often dictated by your schedule.
    2. You may have a gap in employment that allows intensive learning, but only for a month.
    3. Alternatively, you may have a full schedule that permits you to devote only a few hours per week to learning something new.
    4. Whatever time you can commit, decide on it in advance.
- **When you are going to learn.**
    1. **Do whatever makes it easiest based on your schedule.**
    2. Set a consistent schedule that is the same every week, rather than trying to fit in learning when you can.
    3. *Consistency breeds good habits, reducing the effort required to study.*
    4. If you have absolutely no choice, an ad hoc schedule is better than none, but it will require more discipline to sustain.
    5. If you have some flexibility in your schedule, you may want to optimize it.
    6. **Shorter, spaced time chunks are better for memory than crammed chunks are.**
    7. However, some types of tasks, such as writing and programming, have a long warm-up time that may benefit from longer uninterrupted time chunks.
    8. The best way to find out what is best for you is to practice; if you find it takes a long time to warm up, opt for longer spaces in your schedule.
    9. If you find you can get to work within a few minutes of starting, shorter chunks of time spread out will be helpful for long-term retention.
- **Length of time for your project.**
    1. Prefer shorter commitments to longer ones because they are easier to stick with.
    2. An intensive project that lasts a month has fewer potential interruptions from life or from your motivation changing and waning.
    3. If you have a big goal you want to accomplish that can’t be done in a short time frame, break it up into multiple smaller ones of a few months each.

Take all this information and put it into your calendar.

- Benefits
    - Logistically
        - Will help you spot potential conflicts in your schedule due to vacations, work, or family events.
    - Psychologically
        - Will help you remember and act on your initial plan better than if it were written on a piece of paper tucked into a desk drawer.
        - The act of scheduling demonstrates your seriousness about doing the project.
        - If you’re unwilling to put time into your calendar, you’re almost certainly unwilling to put in time to study.
        - If you’re waffling at this stage, that’s a good sign your heart isn’t really in the right place to get started.

If you're embarking on longer projects of **six months or more**, do a **pilot week** of your schedule.

- This is simple: test your schedule for one week before you commit to it.
- This will give you firsthand knowledge of how difficult it will be and prevents overconfidence.
- If you already feel burned out after the first week, make adjustments.
- There’s no shame in going back and retooling your plan to make it fit your life better.
- Making this kind of adjustment is a lot better than giving up midway because your plan was doomed from the start.
