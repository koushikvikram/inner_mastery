## Part 2: Answer Key

1. **What is the core structure of the "Feel Better in 5" program?**  
   The program requires five minutes of time, three times a day, for five days a week. Participants choose one "health snack" from three categories—Mind, Body, and Heart—to integrate into their daily routine.

2. **Why does the author argue that relying solely on willpower and motivation often leads to failure in health plans?**  
   The author asserts that willpower and motivation are finite resources that naturally fluctuate, making them unreliable for long-term change. Most successful programs must instead rely on making behaviors easy and automatic so they do not require significant mental effort.

3. **Explain the concept of "destination thinking" as described in the text.**  
   Destination thinking focuses only on the final result or the symptoms of a problem, such as a specific weight goal or a headache, rather than the daily journey that led there. The author argues that health is a collection of thousands of small daily steps, and focusing on the "journey" is the only way to reach a healthy "destination."

4. **What is the "Ripple Effect" and how does it amplify the results of the program?**  
   The Ripple Effect occurs when small changes in one area of life naturally trigger positive adjustments in other areas, such as a five-minute exercise routine leading to better food choices. Because the mind and body are connected, improving one small part of a person's life causes benefits to spread throughout their entire system.

5. **What are "health snacks" and what three specific areas of health do they target?**  
   Health snacks are simple, five-minute practices designed to be easy to follow and maintain without requiring much willpower. They target the Mind (reducing stress and anxiety), the Body (increasing physical movement), and the Heart (strengthening essential human connections).

6. **According to the behavior change science mentioned, why is it essential to "Start Easy" when forming new habits?**  
   Starting with very low-effort tasks, like flossing just one tooth, ensures that the behavior requires almost no motivation to complete. This allows the action to become habitual and automatic over time, eventually leading to more significant changes without the feeling of a "chore."

7. **How can a person use "anchoring" to ensure they complete their health snacks daily?**  
   Anchoring involves connecting a new health snack to an existing, automatic habit, such as brewing a morning cup of tea or walking through the front door after work. By "hitching" the new behavior to a pre-established routine, the individual does not need to find extra time or remember to do it.

8. **List three ways the author suggests "designing your environment" on your smartphone to reduce mindless usage.**  
   The author suggests changing the phone screen to grayscale to make it less visually stimulating, removing social media apps so they can only be accessed via a browser, and turning off face or fingerprint scanners to force the manual entry of a PIN.

9. **What role does celebration play in the habit-formation process, and what is one visual method suggested to track it?**  
   Positive emotions felt during celebration help lock in a behavior as an automatic habit by providing a hit of pleasure. One suggested visual method is dropping a coffee bean into a glass jar every time a snack is completed to create a tangible representation of success.

10. **How does the program suggest "respecting your rhythm" when scheduling a Body health snack?**  
    The program suggests aligning activities with biological signals, such as performing strength exercises in the afternoon when muscular strength often peaks. Alternatively, doing a snack when cortisol is high in the morning can help change how the body processes blood sugar during breakfast.

## Part 4: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term                  | Definition                                                                                                                     |
|-----------------------|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| **360-Degree Approach**   | A holistic health strategy that simultaneously addresses the Mind, Body, and Heart to ensure the entire system is supported. |
| **Anchoring**             | The technique of attaching a new habit to an existing, automatic daily routine to increase the likelihood of success.         |
| **BDNF**                  | A brain hormone increased by exercise that helps create new nerve connections and may improve mood.                        |
| **Cortisol**              | A stress hormone that, when elevated for long periods, can increase blood sugar and contribute to various health problems.  |
| **Destination Thinking**  | An ineffective approach to health that focuses solely on symptoms or distant goals rather than daily habits.                 |
| **Feel Better Days**      | The five days per week during which a participant commits to performing their three chosen health snacks.                   |
| **Health Snack**          | A five-minute practice intended to improve physical, mental, or emotional wellbeing that is easy to perform and maintain.   |
| **Melatonin**             | A sleep-inducing hormone that peaks in the evening; it can be supported by calming "Heart" snacks before bed.              |
| **Positive Self-Talk**    | The practice of using encouraging, journey-focused language (e.g., "not now" instead of "I can't") to improve willpower and self-compassion. |
| **Ripple Effect**         | The phenomenon where a small positive change in one area of life triggers a chain reaction of improvements in other areas.  |
| **Tiny Habits**           | A behavior change concept (popularized by B.J. Fogg) emphasizing that making a task as small as possible makes it easier to become a habit. |
| **Willpower Barrier**     | The assumption that lasting change requires high levels of grit; the program aims to bypass this by making habits effortless. |
