# Study Guide: Becoming a Key Person of Influence

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the principles and methodologies outlined in the revised edition of *Key Person of Influence* by Daniel Priestley. It explores the transition from the Industrial Age to the digital economy and details the five-step method for becoming a highly valued leader in any industry.

---

6.  **How does a "social pitch" differ from a "presentation pitch"?**
7.  **Why is "The Big Game" central to a person's mission as a Key Person of Influence?**
8.  **What is the difference between an "Information Product" and a "Productised Service"?**
9.  **What does it mean to be "web famous" or "Google-able" in a professional context?**
10. **In the context of the "Partnership" step, what is meant by the "spirit of intent"?**

## Answer Key

1.  **Functional vs. Vital:** Functional people perform replaceable roles based on processes and are often fearful of being overlooked or replaced by technology. Vital people see themselves as an irreplaceable "life-force" aligned with results rather than processes, focusing on creating unique value and adapting dynamically to change.
2.  **The Death of the Career:** A career is described as "old technology" from an era where companies invested in long-term staff development. In the new economy, workers are expected to constantly educate themselves and will likely hold ten to fourteen jobs by age thirty-eight, moving toward "work on demand" structures similar to Hollywood film projects.
3.  **The Inner Circle:** Every industry has an inner circle of well-known, highly valued individuals who curate the best opportunities and share them amongst themselves. Those outside this circle are left to fight over the poorer opportunities that the Key People of Influence (KPIs) have already turned down.
4.  **Mountain of Value:** This concept suggests that every individual is already standing on a unique accumulation of experiences, skills, and stories that have immense value. However, most people fail to recognize this because they are "too close" to it, only seeing the dirt at their feet while admiring the "mountains" of others from a distance.
5.  **The Five Steps and Order:** The steps are Pitch, Publish, Product, Profile, and Partnership. The order is vital because you cannot successfully leverage partnerships if you lack a clear pitch, have no published credibility, lack a scalable business model, and remain invisible online.
6.  **Social vs. Presentation Pitch:** A social pitch is a short, emotionally charged response to "What do you do?" designed to hook interest in under two minutes. A presentation pitch is a structured, rehearsed delivery (ranging from minutes to hours) that explains the problem, the solution, the payoff, and a specific call to action.
7.  **The Big Game:** "The Big Game" is a person's core mission or "why"—the reason they get up in the morning. It must have rules, players, a prize, and a way to win or lose, serving as the heart of how one designs their business and delivers an irresistible experience to others.
8.  **Information Product vs. Productised Service:** An information product (like a podcast or eBook) packages ideas to educate a high volume of people and build relationships. A productised service takes a bespoke service and standardizes elements of its delivery, naming it and creating a method around it so it can be scaled as a high-value implementation solution.
9.  **Web Famous:** Being "web famous" or "Google-able" means that when someone searches for your name, they find coherent social media profiles, videos, articles, and reviews. This online presence serves as a "microphone" that proves your credibility and allows potential partners to trust you before a deal even begins.
10. **Spirit of Intent:** This refers to approaching partnerships with a genuine desire to see the other party succeed as much as oneself. It involves working as hard for the partner’s wins as for one’s own and avoiding the impulse to "squeeze every drop" out of a relationship.

## Essay Questions

**Instructions:** Use the principles discussed in the source text to provide detailed responses to the following prompts.

1.  **The Shift in Economic Eras:** Discuss the transition from the Industrial Age to the Digital Economy. How have the "means of production" changed, and why does this empower small, lean teams over traditional large corporations?
2.  **The Logic of Micro-Niches:** Explain why a "general store" approach to business is ineffective in a geographically unlimited world. How does claiming a micro-niche create more wealth and power for a Key Person of Influence?
3.  **The Multiplier Effect of Products:** Analyze the statement, "Products and services don’t make money; product and service ecosystems do." Describe how a KPI can move from "trading time for money" to creating a scalable ecosystem.
4.  **The Role of Content as Ownership:** Compare a "Title Deed" in the agricultural age to published content in the information age. Why is the act of publishing a book or articles considered a primary driver of industry "authority"?
5.  **Embracing Critics and Feedback:** The text suggests that "That won't work!" is one of the best responses to a pitch. Explain the strategic value of negative feedback and how a KPI can use it to refine their message and business model.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Key Person of Influence (KPI)** | A well-connected, well-known, and highly valued individual at the center of their industry who attracts and curates the best opportunities. |
| **Micro-niche** | A highly specialized sub-category within a larger industry where an individual can establish themselves as the dominant authority. |
| **Functional Person** | An individual who performs replaceable tasks and focuses on processes; they are often "looking good but going nowhere." |
| **Vital Person** | An individual who is aligned with results and seen as an irreplaceable "life-force" within a project or industry. |
| **Intellectual Property (IP)** | Packaged-up ideas or insights that have been turned into assets, such as books, methods, or digital products. |
| **Product Ecosystem** | A mix of free, high-volume, and high-value products/services that work together to maximize the value of customer relationships. |
| **The Big Game** | The overarching mission or "why" behind a KPI's work, providing the emotional fuel and structural rules for their professional activities. |
| **Swagman** | A metaphor for an itinerant worker who wanders between projects without ever staking a claim to a specific niche or "land." |
| **Presentation Pitch** | A structured, rehearsed explanation of an idea that includes the problem, the solution, proof of value, and a specific request. |
| **Implementation Solution** | High-value products or services that focus on "doing" the work for the client, as opposed to simply providing information. |
| **Polite Response** | A benign reaction (e.g., "Sounds interesting") that indicates a lack of true engagement; the opposite of the emotionally charged reaction a KPI seeks. |
| **Multi-Niche Scaling** | The process of starting in a tiny micro-niche (like Facebook at Harvard) and expanding to larger territories once notoriety is achieved. |
