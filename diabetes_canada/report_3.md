# Comprehensive Briefing on Diabetes Prevention and Management

## Executive Summary

Effective diabetes management and prevention rely on a multi-faceted approach centered on balanced nutrition, regular physical activity, and diligent monitoring. Key strategies include utilizing the "Plate Method" for portion control—emphasizing a diet rich in vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins—and achieving at least 150 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous physical activity per week. For those with South Asian dietary preferences, specific substitutions (such as whole wheat chapati for white rice) and cooking modifications (baking instead of frying) are critical. Successful long-term management is further supported by SMART goal setting, carbohydrate counting, and strategic planning when dining away from home to maintain blood glucose levels within target ranges.

---

## 1. Understanding Diabetes and Blood Glucose Target Ranges

Diabetes is a condition where the body cannot properly use or store glucose, a form of sugar derived from carbohydrates. When glucose is not used effectively for energy, it builds up in the blood.

### Types of Diabetes and Treatment
| Type | Description | Treatment Focus |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Type 1** | Pancreas produces no insulin. | Insulin injections and healthy eating. |
| **Type 2** | Pancreas produces insufficient insulin or the body resists it. | Healthy eating, activity, pills, and/or insulin. |
| **Pre-diabetes** | Elevated blood glucose, but below Type 2 threshold. | Healthy eating and physical activity. |
| **Gestational** | High blood sugar occurring during pregnancy. | Healthy eating; may require insulin or pills. |

### Blood Glucose Targets
For most individuals, maintaining blood glucose within the following ranges is recommended:
*   **Before meals:** 4 to 7 mmol/L.
*   **2 hours after the start of a meal:** 5 to 10 mmol/L.

## 2. Nutritional Management Strategies

### The Plate Method
This method provides a visual guide for balancing nutrients and managing portions:
*   **½ Plate:** Vegetables and fruits (emphasizing brightly colored varieties).
*   **¼ Plate:** Whole grain products and starchy vegetables (e.g., brown rice, whole wheat roti, potatoes).
*   **¼ Plate:** Protein foods (e.g., dahl, fish, lean chicken, tofu, paneer).

### The Handy Portion Guide
Individuals can use their hands to estimate appropriate serving sizes:
*   **Grains/Starches or Fruits:** An amount the size of a fist.
*   **Vegetables:** As much as can be held in both hands.
*   **Protein Foods:** The size of the palm and the thickness of the little finger.
*   **Fats:** Limited to the size of the tip of the thumb.
*   **Milk/Alternatives:** Up to 1 cup (250 mL) of low-fat milk.

### South Asian Dietary Specifics
| Category | Healthier Choices | Foods to Limit |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Grains** | Whole wheat chapati, basmati or brown rice, whole grain roti. | White rice, parathas, puris, white bread. |
| **Proteins** | Dahl, chickpeas, lentils, tofu, fish, skinless poultry, eggs. | High-fat/salty meats (sausages, wings, pork ribs). |
| **Snacks/Sides** | Salad, upma, papad, roasted chickpeas, nuts, yogurt. | Samosas, pakoras, kachori, namkeen. |
| **Desserts** | Fresh fruit, low-fat yogurt, lassi (low-fat). | Mithai, jalebi, ladoo, kheer, gulab jamun. |
| **Fats** | Canola, olive, or peanut oil. | Ghee, butter, coconut oil, animal fats. |

## 3. Carbohydrate Counting and Monitoring

Carbohydrates have the greatest impact on blood glucose. Consistency in carbohydrate intake from meal to meal is vital for stability.

### Daily Guidelines
*   **Women:** 45 to 60 grams per meal; 15 to 30 grams per snack.
*   **Men:** 60 to 75 grams per meal; 15 to 30 grams per snack.

### Reading Nutrition Facts
To determine "available carbohydrate," subtract the fibre grams from the total carbohydrate grams, as fibre does not raise blood sugar.
*   *Calculation:* Total Carbohydrate - Fibre = Available Carbohydrate.

## 4. Strategic Dining Away from Home

Eating out requires proactive planning to avoid excessive portions and hidden fats or sugars.

*   **Menu Planning:** Review menus online in advance to check carbohydrate counts.
*   **Special Requests:** Ask for items to be baked instead of fried, and request sauces, dressings, and gravies on the side to control fat intake.
*   **Portion Control:** Request half portions or immediately pack half of the meal to take home. Resist "upsizing" offers.
*   **Cooking Methods:** Prioritize tandoor-cooked, steamed, roasted, broiled, or grilled items over fried dishes. Ask for oil to be substituted for ghee.
*   **Buffet Strategies:** Browse all options before selecting. Go through the line only once, prioritizing vegetables and following the Plate Method.
*   **Timing:** Avoid skipping meals before an event, as this often leads to overeating later.

## 5. Physical Activity and Lifestyle Integration

Physical activity improves insulin sensitivity and prevents glucose buildup. Diabetes Canada recommends 150 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous aerobic activity per week.

### Activity Categories
1.  **Aerobic Exercise:** Continuous movement that raises heart rate (e.g., brisk walking, biking, swimming).
2.  **Resistance Exercise:** Building muscle strength 2–3 times per week using weights, bands, or body weight.
3.  **Interval Training:** Alternating short bursts of vigorous activity with recovery periods.

### Top Tips to Reduce Sedentary Time
*   Set a timer for mini-movement breaks (e.g., stand/stretch for 3 minutes every 30 minutes).
*   Stand up while taking phone calls or during TV commercials.
*   Embrace "inconvenience" by moving frequently used items further away.
*   Choose active transport: walk to errands, take stairs, or park further away.
*   Perform simple exercises like balancing on one leg while waiting for a kettle or microwave.

### Overcoming Common Barriers
*   **Lack of Time:** Start with 5–10 minute sessions; every minute counts.
*   **Fatigue:** Activity often increases energy levels and improves sleep.
*   **Cost:** Gym memberships are unnecessary; walking and household chores are effective.
*   **Fear of Lows:** Monitor blood sugar before and after activity and carry fast-acting carbohydrates (e.g., hard candy or glucose tablets).

## 6. Behavioral Change and Goal Setting

Sustainable lifestyle changes are achieved through structured planning.

*   **Decisional Balance:** Weigh the pros and cons of staying the same versus making a change.
*   **SMART Goals:**
    *   **Specific:** "Walk 35 minutes a day" rather than "walk more."
    *   **Measurable:** Quantifiable targets (e.g., "Lose 2 kg").
    *   **Attainable:** Realistic steps that build confidence.
    *   **Relevant:** Tied to personal motivations (e.g., "be healthy for my children").
    *   **Time-oriented:** Setting a deadline (e.g., "within 2 months").

## 7. Sugars, Sweeteners, and Alcohol

### Sweetener Management
*   **Nutritive Sugars:** Includes honey, maple syrup, and agave. These must be eaten in moderation (maximum 10% of daily calories) as they increase blood glucose.
*   **Sugar Alcohols:** Found in "sugar-free" products (e.g., sorbitol, xylitol). They have little effect on blood sugar but can cause bloating or diarrhea if intake exceeds 10 grams per day.
*   **Non-Nutritive Sweeteners:** Aspartame, Sucralose, Acesulfame Potassium, and Stevia are Health Canada-approved and do not increase blood sugar.

### Alcohol Consumption
Alcohol can cause blood glucose levels to fluctuate significantly (too high or too low) and may interfere with medications. It is also calorie-dense and can contribute to weight gain. Consult a healthcare provider to determine safe consumption limits.
