# Study Guide: I Have the Streets – A Kutti Cricket Story

This study guide provides a comprehensive overview of the early life and career of Indian cricketer R. Ashwin as detailed in the source context. It explores his formative years in Madras, his struggle with health and injury, the influence of his family, and his evolution from a street-cricket enthusiast to a professional offspinner.

## Part II: Answer Key

1.  **Ashwin’s Health Challenges:** As a young child, Ashwin was diagnosed with a primary complex in his lungs (childhood tuberculosis) and later suffered from malaria. These conditions left him with frequent wheezing attacks and a tendency to vomit after physical exertion, forcing him to balance a high drive for play with chronic physical weakness.
2.  **Ramakrishnapuram Street Cricket:** The games used a lamp post as a striker's wicket and a stone at the non-striker’s end for "current" run-outs. Scoring was restricted to the off-side to protect windows, and hitting the ball over walls into houses resulted in a dismissal.
3.  **Appa’s Academic Management:** Appa was a strict disciplinarian who personally studied Ashwin's textbooks and copied notes from classmates’ houses. He administered three-hour mock exams with a clock and used reasoning and arguments to ensure Ashwin maintained high scores to satisfy their orthodox extended family.
4.  **The Hip Injury:** At fourteen, Ashwin suffered a slipped hip disc that initially led a doctor to recommend two surgeries and the installation of screws, which likely would have ended his cricket career. Instead, he underwent six weeks of total bed rest followed by months of restricted activity, during which he gained significant weight.
5.  **Amma’s Influence on Spin:** While Ashwin was recovering from his injury and avoiding fast bowling to protect his leg, Amma suggested he "do something else" rather than just standing in the field. Despite Appa's technical reservations, she encouraged him to experiment with off-spin so he could remain active in the game even if he got out early as a batter.
6.  **India Under-17 Alienation:** Ashwin felt humiliated and intimidated because the camp was dominated by North Indian players who spoke only Hindi dialects, which he did not understand. He struggled to translate his thoughts from Tamil into "bookish" Hindi, leading the other players to mock him for being a "Madrasi" and for his dedication to his studies.
7.  **D. Vasu’s Mentorship:** Vasu, the captain of Alwarpet CC, taught Ashwin that wicket-taking is an art separate from just bowling with control. He mentored Ashwin on how to set fields, how to use "topspinners" to target specific batters' weaknesses, and the importance of never making the batter feel comfortable.
8.  **Discovery of the Sodakku:** The "sodakku" (carrom ball) is a finger-flicked delivery that turns unpredictably; Ashwin first saw it used with a hard cricket ball by a Sri Lankan youth at a V.V. Kumar spin camp. He later saw the professional Rangana Herath use it during the M.J. Gopalan Trophy, confirming it was a viable weapon for top-level cricket.
9.  **W.V. Raman’s Philosophy:** Raman taught Ashwin that a spinner must master length, line, and revolutions (revs). He argued that while "revs" are involuntary, controlling the "line" by running in a straight line is the most critical factor because it allows for a greater margin of error in "length."
10. **Refusal of Nets Bowling:** Ashwin felt slighted because international players like Chris Gayle and the West Indies team provided no eye contact or acknowledgement, even when he bowled them out. He realized he did not want to be a "blue-collar worker" for stars and vowed to return only when he was good enough to be considered one of their peers.

## Part IV: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **12B** | A prominent bus route in Madras; also the title of a Tamil film reflecting the parallel paths a life can take based on minor events. |
| **4Aces** | A specific type of tennis ball used in Madras street cricket that travels further than a standard tennis ball. |
| **Amma** | Mother (Tamil). |
| **Appa** | Father (Tamil). |
| **Chepauk** | The common name for the MA Chidambaram Stadium in Madras, known for its knowledgeable crowd and the smell of pan masala. |
| **Days Cricket** | Multiple-day matches (like Ranji Trophy) with no limit on the number of overs a team can bat, emphasizing endurance and strategy. |
| **Doosra** | A delivery that looks like an offbreak but turns the other way; often controversial due to the required arm action. |
| **Gully Cricket** | Street cricket played in narrow lanes, often characterized by unique local rules and high levels of competitiveness. |
| **Jolly Rovers** | An elite first-division club team in Madras owned by Chemplast, considered the pinnacle of local league cricket. |
| **Madrasi** | A term often used disparagingly by those from North India to describe anyone from the South; Ashwin reclaims this as a point of pride. |
| **Mankading** | The act of a bowler running out the non-striker for leaving the crease too early; named after Vinoo Mankad. |
| **NCA** | National Cricket Academy in Bangalore, where top-tier Indian youth players undergo intensive training and fitness testing. |
| **Primary Complex** | A medical term for childhood tuberculosis, which Ashwin suffered from at age five. |
| **Sodakku** | Tamil for "click" or "snap"; refers to the carrom ball, a delivery flicked with the middle finger to deceive the batter. |
| **Thatha** | Grandfather (Tamil). |
| **TRDW** | Talent Research Development Wing; a BCCI initiative to identify and groom promising young cricketers. |
