# Epictetus Operating Model — Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (TherapyAid Toolkit)

> Source frame: mental health is **maintenance**, not a destination — the same consistency as physical fitness. The CBT operating stack: (1) **emotions as data** — anger is the visible tip of the **Anger Iceberg** masking hurt, shame, anxiety, grief, or even hunger/tiredness; triggers and physical **warning signs** (sweating, clenched fists, raised voice) are early-intervention signals; (2) **physiology before reasoning** — at escalation, don't argue; deploy the **4-4-6 breath** (inhale 4s, pause 4s, exhale 6s) or a **30-minute diversion** / **30–60 minute time-out** until the nervous system returns to baseline; (3) **cognitive restructuring** — depression/anxiety run on irrational thoughts; put them on trial with evidence checks, the **friend test**, and **"will this matter in 1–5 years?"** (**Helicopter View**: fact vs opinion); (4) **behavioral activation** — schedule positive experiences with **incremental goals** when motivation is low; (5) **self-care as boundary** — regular meals, sleep, hygiene, and saying "no" are non-negotiable maintenance, not indulgence (substance use is avoidant, not self-care); (6) **protective factors + maintenance plan** — triggers, warning signs, self-care habits, and pre-rehearsed coping strategies practiced *before* crisis. Reframe **stress as a tool** in small doses; knock out small tasks first to clear mental space. The unit of control is **intervene at the warning sign**, not after the iceberg surfaces.

## Layer 1 — Practice (What must be done?)

### What we’re trying to achieve
- Treat emotions as **information**, not interruptions — read the iceberg beneath anger.
- Intervene **proactively** at triggers and warning signs before states become unmanageable.
- Restructure irrational thoughts through evidence-based scrutiny and perspective.
- Maintain mental health through **structured self-care** and pre-rehearsed coping skills.
- Build **protective factors** (social support, physical health, purpose, healthy thinking).

### Non-negotiable behaviors
- **Look beneath anger**: name the primary emotion (hurt, shame, grief) — don't stop at the tip.
- **Catch warning signs early**: physical (hot, clenched fists) and behavioral (arguing, shutting down).
- **Don't reason while aroused**: use 4-4-6 breathing, a 30-minute diversion, or a time-out first.
- **Challenge negative thoughts**: evidence for/against, friend test, 1–5 year test, fact vs opinion.
- **Schedule positive activity**: behavioral activation with incremental goals when motivation drops.
- **Practice self-care as maintenance**: even when feeling good — boundaries and basics are non-negotiable.
- **Rehearse coping before crisis**: skills live in the maintenance plan, not in the emergency.

### Routines / checklists

- **Anger Iceberg drill (when triggered)**
  - Name the trigger (person, place, situation).
  - Scan warning signs — am I in the escalation zone?
  - Ask: *what's beneath the anger?* (disappointed, hurt, ashamed, tired, hungry, overwhelmed).
  - If aroused: 4-4-6 breath OR 30-minute diversion OR agreed time-out (30–60 min).

- **4-4-6 breathing**
  - Inhale through nose for 4 seconds (abdomen rises).
  - Pause 4 seconds.
  - Exhale slowly through puckered lips for 6 seconds.

- **Cognitive challenge (Helicopter View)**
  - Is there substantial evidence for or against this thought?
  - Am I interpreting without all the evidence?
  - What would a friend think?
  - Is this fact or opinion?
  - Will this matter in 1 year? 5 years?

- **Behavioral activation (weekly schedule)**
  - Start simple (wake by 8, eat breakfast) → progress to social contact, hobbies, physical activity.
  - Schedule diverse activities: movement, connection, mastery.

- **Gratitude journal (2×/week)**
  - Write detailed entries (10–20 min) focusing on *people* and *why* you're grateful — not just lists.

- **Mental health maintenance plan (four pillars)**
  - **Triggers**: what revives issues — manage or avoid.
  - **Warning signs**: sleep disruption, isolation, irritability — act when they appear.
  - **Self-care habits**: exercise, socializing, basics — maintain when well.
  - **Coping strategies**: breathing, communication, relaxation — practice regularly.

- **Stress reframe + prioritization**
  - Reframe stress as a tool/motivator in small doses — not an enemy to eliminate.
  - Clear small quick tasks first to free mental space for larger responsibilities.

### What good execution looks like
- Triggers and warning signs are named before full escalation.
- Anger episodes get iceberg analysis and physiological reset before discussion.
- Negative thoughts are routinely challenged — not believed on first appearance.
- Activity is scheduled even when unmotivated; momentum builds incrementally.
- Self-care and coping skills are practiced in calm periods, not invented in crisis.
- Protective factors (support, health, purpose, thinking) are actively maintained.

## Layer 2 — Principles (Why does it work?)

- **Anger is often secondary**: the visible emotion masks vulnerability — treating the tip misses the cause.
- **Early intervention beats late repair**: warning signs are cheaper to act on than full meltdowns.
- **Arousal blocks cognition**: reasoning while physiologically escalated fails — reset biology first.
- **Thoughts are not facts**: irrational negative thoughts drive depression/anxiety; scrutiny breaks the loop.
- **Behavior precedes motivation**: scheduling activity generates positive experiences that restore mood.
- **Self-care is preventive medicine**: maintaining when well prevents collapse when stressed.
- **Stress in small doses motivates**: eliminating all stress is impossible; reframing reduces harm.
- **Protective factors are buildable**: social support, coping skills, and healthy thinking are controllable.

### Decision rules (doctrine)
- If anger rises, look beneath it — don't react to the tip alone.
- If warning signs appear, intervene now — don't wait for full escalation.
- If you're clenched, hot, or shouting, **don't reason** — breathe, divert, or time-out first.
- If a thought feels catastrophic, run the evidence/friend/5-year/Helicopter tests.
- If motivation is zero, behavioral activation — smallest scheduled win first.
- If you're "too busy" for self-care, that's when the maintenance plan matters most.
- If stress feels overwhelming, clear small tasks and reframe — then tackle the macro issue.

## Layer 3 — Meta (How do we evaluate and improve?)

### Metrics (leading, behavior-tied)
- **Early-catch rate**: interventions at warning-sign stage vs full escalation.
- **Iceberg depth**: primary emotions identified beneath anger episodes.
- **Thought challenges / week**: negative thoughts put through the scrutiny protocol.
- **Behavioral activation adherence**: scheduled positive activities completed.
- **Self-care consistency**: basics (sleep, meals, boundaries) maintained when feeling good.
- **Gratitude entries**: 2 detailed entries per week.
- **Protective factor audit**: social support, physical health, purpose, healthy thinking — quarterly.

### Review loop
- **After anger/stress episode**: anger log — trigger, thoughts, reaction, what was beneath, what worked.
- **Weekly**: maintenance plan check — any warning signs? Coping skills practiced?
- **Monthly**: protective factors review — where is support, health, or thinking weak?
- **Quarterly**: update triggers, warning signs, and coping strategies from patterns in logs.

### Core rule guardrail
- If you're repeatedly "surprised" by blow-ups, you're ignoring **warning signs** and treating anger as primary instead of reading the **iceberg**. Restore the maintenance plan — triggers, early signals, self-care, and pre-rehearsed coping — because mental health is maintained in calm periods, not invented in crisis.

---

## Operating Sequence
1. When triggered, name the warning signs and look beneath anger for the primary emotion.
2. If aroused, reset physiology — 4-4-6 breath, 30-minute diversion, or time-out — before reasoning.
3. Challenge irrational thoughts with evidence, the friend test, and the Helicopter View.
4. Schedule and execute behavioral activation; maintain self-care as a boundary, not an indulgence.
5. Run the four-pillar maintenance plan weekly and update it from anger logs and protective-factor audits.
