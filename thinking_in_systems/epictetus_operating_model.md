# Epictetus Operating Model — Donella Meadows, *Thinking in Systems*


![Epictetus Operating Model — Donella Meadows, *Thinking in Systems*](./epictetus_operating_model.png)

> Source frame: a system is a set of **stocks** (accumulations) connected by **flows** (rates) and governed by **feedback loops** — *balancing* loops that seek a goal and *reinforcing* loops that amplify. Behavior is **latent in structure** (the Slinky bounces because of its coils, not the hand), so most problems are "messes" that won't yield to an external fix. **Delays** between action and effect cause overshoot and oscillation; **bounded rationality** means rational local actors produce collective dysfunction. Systems fall into recurring **traps** — policy resistance, tragedy of the commons, drift to low performance, success-to-the-successful, shifting the burden (addiction), rule-beating, seeking the wrong goal — each with a structural way out. The highest-yield interventions are **leverage points**, and Meadows' counterintuitive ranking puts *numbers* dead last and *paradigms / transcending paradigms, goals, rules, information flows, and self-organization* near the top. The endgame is not control but to **"dance with the system."**

## Layer 1 — Practice (What must be done?)

### What we’re trying to achieve
- See problems as **structures**, not events or villains — find the spring, not the hand.
- Intervene at **high leverage** (goals, rules, information, paradigms) instead of "diddling with the details" (numbers).
- Recognize and escape **system traps** by their structural signatures.
- Manage **delays** and **bounded rationality** rather than being surprised by them.

### Non-negotiable behaviors
- **Map before you fix**: identify the stocks, flows, and feedback loops before pulling any lever.
- **Stop blaming actors**; ask what *structure* makes reasonable people produce the bad outcome.
- **Locate the leverage point** — then check you're pushing it in the right direction (intuition often reverses it).
- **Name the trap** when behavior repeats: which archetype is this, and what's its way out?
- **Respect delays**: don't over-correct before feedback arrives; expect overshoot.

### Routines / checklists

- **System map (before intervening, 10–15 min)**
  - **Stocks**: what is accumulating (trust, cash, skill, fatigue, backlog)?
  - **Flows**: what inflows/outflows change each stock?
  - **Loops**: where are the *balancing* (goal-seeking) and *reinforcing* (amplifying) loops?
  - **Delays**: where does effect lag cause?

- **Leverage-point ladder (rank your intervention, low → high)**
  1. Numbers / parameters (faucets — *dead last*).
  2. Buffer and stock-and-flow structure.
  3. Delays.
  4. Balancing & reinforcing loop strengths.
  5. **Information flows** (who knows what — "move the electric meter").
  6. **Rules** (incentives, constraints).
  7. **Self-organization** (power to add/change structure).
  8. **Goals** of the system.
  9. **Paradigm** (the mindset the system arises from).
  10. **Transcending paradigms** ("not-knowing").
  - Rule: if you're fighting over a number, climb the ladder.

- **Trap diagnosis (when a problem won't stay solved)**
  - **Policy resistance / fixes that fail** → actors pulling to inconsistent goals → *let go; find a shared overarching goal*.
  - **Tragedy of the commons** → missing feedback from resource to user → *educate, privatize, or regulate (mutual coercion mutually agreed)*.
  - **Drift to low performance (boiled frog)** → standards slip with results → *keep standards absolute; peg to best past, not worst*.
  - **Success to the successful** → winners buy more winning → *level the field: antitrust, resets, redistribution*.
  - **Shifting the burden / addiction** → symptom-relief erodes self-capacity → *withdraw the crutch; rebuild the system's own capacity*.
  - **Rule beating** → evasive compliance → *redesign rules to reward their true purpose*.
  - **Seeking the wrong goal** → system obeys a bad metric (GNP, throughput) → *fix the goal/indicator to measure real welfare*.

- **Information-loop scan (cheap high-leverage move)**
  - Where is a decision-maker missing the feedback their action creates?
  - Add the missing loop (make the meter visible; deliver the population data to the investor).

- **Goal check (highest practical lever)**
  - What is the system *actually* optimizing (often "grow / control"), vs. what it claims (e.g., "profit is a rule, not the goal")?
  - If the goal is wrong, no rule below it will hold — change the goal.

### What good execution looks like
- You can sketch the stocks, flows, and at least one balancing and one reinforcing loop for the problem.
- Interventions target rules/information/goals/paradigms, not just numbers.
- Repeating problems get named as a specific trap with its structural exit.
- You build in tolerance for delays instead of over-correcting.
- You optimize for the health of the **stocks** (trust, resilience), not just short-term **throughput**.

## Layer 2 — Principles (Why does it work?)

- **Structure generates behavior**: the bounce is in the Slinky; blame and external fixes miss the source.
- **Numbers are the weakest lever; paradigms the strongest**: 99% of effort goes to faucets that barely move the system.
- **Intuition reverses leverage**: people find the right point and push it the wrong way — verify direction.
- **Bounded rationality is structural, not stupid**: rational local incentives (the herdsman's +1 cow) produce collective ruin; fix the incentive structure, not the people.
- **Delays cause overshoot and oscillation**: feedback that arrives late makes well-meant action destabilizing.
- **Missing feedback breeds tragedy**: connect actors to the consequences of their actions.
- **Systems are obedient to their goals/metrics**: "we get exactly what we measure" — so measure stocks, not throughput.
- **Resilience beats optimization**: over-optimized systems (America's Cup yachts) become unseaworthy; keep slack and self-repair.
- **Mastery is dancing, not controlling**: "strategically, profoundly, madly, letting go and dancing with the system."

### Decision rules (doctrine)
- If a problem keeps "kicking back" → it's structural; stop blaming actors and map the loops.
- If you're fighting over a parameter → climb the leverage ladder to rules, information, or goals.
- If you found a leverage point → pause and check the direction before pushing.
- If a quick fix relieves a symptom → ask what self-capacity it's eroding (shifting the burden).
- If standards are sliding with results → re-peg to best past performance, not last year's drift.
- If the metric is driving destruction → change the goal/indicator, don't tune the number.
- If effort yields oscillation → suspect a delay; slow your corrections.

## Layer 3 — Meta (How do we evaluate and improve?)

### Metrics (leading, structure-tied)
- **Leverage-level of interventions** (where on the ladder you acted — trend upward from numbers toward goals/paradigms).
- **Traps named / escaped** (recurring problems diagnosed by archetype with a structural exit applied).
- **Information loops added / month** (missing feedback connected to decision-makers).
- **Stock health vs. throughput** (are real stocks — trust, skill, resilience — growing, not just flow rates?).
- **Over-correction incidents** (actions taken before delayed feedback arrived — target ↓).

### Review loop
- **Daily / per-decision**: For today's main intervention — which leverage level was it, and did I push it the right way?
- **Weekly**: Which recurring problem is actually a known trap? Apply its way out instead of more force.
- **Monthly**: Are we optimizing throughput at the expense of stocks and resilience? Is any "fix" creating an addiction? What goal or paradigm needs to shift?

### Core rule guardrail
- Don't mistake busywork at the bottom of the ladder for change. Rearranging the deck chairs — fighting over numbers and blaming actors — feels productive while the ship sinks. If a problem persists, the structure is producing it by design: climb to rules, information, goals, and paradigms, verify the direction, and then let go enough to *dance with the system* rather than wrestle it.

---

## Operating Sequence

1. **Map**: identify stocks, flows, and the balancing/reinforcing loops (plus delays) behind the problem.
2. **Stop blaming**: treat the behavior as latent in structure, not caused by a villain or an external hand.
3. **Diagnose the trap**: match repeating problems to an archetype and recall its structural way out.
4. **Climb the leverage ladder**: move from numbers toward information, rules, goals, and paradigms.
5. **Check direction, then act**: confirm you're pushing the leverage point the right way; respect delays.
6. **Tend the stocks and dance**: optimize for resilience and real welfare, review by leverage level, and let go enough to co-evolve with the system.
