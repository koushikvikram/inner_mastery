# Why You Can’t Control the World (And Why You Should Dance With It Instead)

In our modern, industrial world, we are raised with a deep-seated obsession: the quest for prediction and control. We treat the world like a giant machine, believing that if we can just map enough interconnections or harness enough computing power, we can finally master the systems that surround us.

Many people who first encounter systems thinking make a "terrible mistake." They assume that here, in the complexity of interconnections and the rigor of feedback loops, is finally the key to that total control. I assumed that at first, too. We all did, as eager students at MIT. We were enchanted by the lens of systems analysis, believing it would finally make the world "work."

But like explorers searching for a passage to India who ran into the Western Hemisphere instead, we found something we didn't expect. We discovered that systems thinking doesn't lead to a master control room; it leads us straight into the most deeply human mysteries of the heart and soul. Our first "comeuppance" came when we realized that understanding how to fix a system is a world away from actually fixing it. We gave learned lectures on the structure of addiction but couldn't give up coffee. We modeled the dynamics of eroding goals while our own jogging programs fell apart. We warned against the traps of "shifting the burden" even as we created them in our own marriages. 

We realized then that social systems are not just math—they are the external manifestations of human needs, emotions, and weaknesses. As G. K. Chesterton so wisely observed:

> "The real trouble with this world of ours is not that it is an unreasonable world, nor even that it is a reasonable one. The commonest kind of trouble is that it is nearly reasonable, but not quite. Life is not an illogicality; yet it is a trap for logicians. It looks just a little more mathematical and regular than it is."

### The Illusion of the Omniscient Conqueror

The industrial mindset tells us that there is a way to foresee the future exactly and prepare for it perfectly. But this goal is unrealizable. Self-organizing, nonlinear systems are inherently unpredictable. Our science—from quantum theory to the mathematics of chaos—leads us not toward total certainty, but toward irreducible uncertainty.

Admitting that we cannot optimize everything or keep track of every variable is not a failure of intelligence; it is a liberating step toward sanity. For any objective other than the most trivial, we simply cannot "conquer" our way to success. As the source of so much systemic wisdom reminds us:

> "We can’t find a proper, sustainable relationship to nature, each other, or the institutions we create, if we try to do it from the role of omniscient conqueror."

### Stop Controlling, Start Dancing

If we stop being blinded by the illusion of control, a shining alternative emerges. We can’t impose our will on a system, but we can "dance" with it. This isn't just a metaphor for poets; it is a requirement for anyone doing intellectual work, management, or government.

The future cannot be predicted, but it can be envisioned and brought lovingly into being. Dancing with a system means staying wide awake and participating "flat out." I learned this in whitewater kayaking and gardening, where you don't command the river or the soil; you listen to what they tell you and respond. Living successfully in a world of systems requires our full humanity—not just our ability to calculate, but our intuition, our compassion, our vision, and our morality.

### Get the Beat: The Art of Watching Before Acting

The first lesson in this dance is to "get the beat" of the system. Before you disturb anything, watch how it behaves. Focus on facts, not theories. It is startling how often our "common sense" theories are flatly contradicted by reality. People will swear that rainfall is decreasing when, in fact, the data shows that variability is simply increasing—the droughts are deeper, but the floods are greater.

We must rely on actual data and time graphs because human memory is notoriously unreliable when it comes to timing. Watching what really happens, rather than listening to theories of what happens, explodes many careless hypotheses. For instance, while many believe town growth lowers taxes, plotting the data often reveals a scatter as random as the stars in a winter sky. 

To find the beat, we should start asking dynamic questions: How did we get here? Where are we going if we don't change direction? And crucially, what is working well right now? This prevents us from defining a problem simply by the absence of our favorite solution.

### The Information Commandment: Thou Shalt Not Distort

Information is the light that allows a system to see itself. When it is delayed, biased, or missing, the system runs amok. If I could, I would add an eleventh commandment to the original ten: *Thou shalt not distort, delay, or withhold information.*

You can make a system work better with surprising ease just by restoring information streams. Consider the 1986 Toxic Release Inventory. The law simply required companies to report their legal chemical emissions to the public. There were no fines, no lawsuits, and no new regulations. Yet, within two years, emissions nationwide decreased by 40%. Some companies slashed emissions by 90% just because that previously withheld information was now visible in the local papers. Information is power; when you muddy its stream, you drive the system crazy.

### Beyond the Spreadsheet: The Power of the Unquantifiable

Our culture is obsessed with numbers, operating under the delusion that if you can’t measure it, it doesn't matter. But leaving out "hard-to-measure" variables like quality, justice, or love is fundamentally unscientific. 

In one study on minority workers, researchers found that "prejudice"—though difficult to quantify—had a massive, nonlinear impact on performance. To ignore it would have been a failure of logic. We must refuse the ploy that says we don't have to pay attention to what we can't define or measure. 

Instead, we must become "quality detectors"—walking, noisy Geiger counters that register the presence of beauty, truth, and democracy. No one can measure justice or freedom, but if no one speaks up for them, and if our systems aren't designed to produce them, they will cease to exist.

### Intrinsic Responsibility: Putting the Pilot in the Front

A system is most resilient when it is designed for "intrinsic responsibility"—meaning the consequences of a decision return directly and quickly to the decision-maker. The classic example is a pilot: because they sit in the front of the plane, they experience the results of their choices immediately.

Modern culture often moves in the opposite direction, distancing us from the consequences of our actions. I saw this at Dartmouth College when they took thermostats out of individual offices and handed control to a central computer to save energy. The result? Massive oscillations in temperature. Being at a "low level in the hierarchy," I had to call an office across campus when I was freezing. By the time they responded, they would overcorrect, and I'd be sweltering hours later. 

Designing for responsibility means placing a factory's intake pipes downstream from its own outflow pipes. When the feedback is direct and compelling, the system learns to care for itself.

### Error-Embracing: Staying a Learner

In a complex system, "stay the course" is often the most dangerous advice you can follow. If you are on an unknown, obstacle-strewn path, you don't charge forward with rigid directives. You take small steps, monitor the results, and change course as you learn.

Psychologist Don Michael calls this "error-embracing." It is the opposite of the "bluffing and freezing" we see in so many of our institutions. It takes immense courage to admit we were wrong, but acknowledging uncertainty is the only way to reverse the growing trend of distrust in authority. As the wisdom of systems reminds us:

> "The very act of acknowledging uncertainty could help greatly to reverse this worsening trend... Error-embracing is the condition for learning."

### Conclusion: The Boundary of Caring

Ultimately, systems thinking leads us to a realization about the human spirit: the real system is interconnected. There are no separate systems; the world is a continuum. 

This means that moral behavior is actually the most practical behavior. It is not possible in this integrated world for your heart to succeed if your lungs fail. The rich cannot succeed if the poor fail; the global economy cannot succeed if the global environment fails; and Europe cannot succeed if Africa fails. 

Living successfully in a world of systems requires us to expand our horizons of caring. It is a call to move beyond the desire for conquest and toward a more harmonious participation. Are we brave enough to stop trying to conquer our systems and finally learn how to dance with them?
