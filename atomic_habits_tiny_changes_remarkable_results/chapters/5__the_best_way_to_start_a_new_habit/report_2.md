## Part 2: Answer Key

1.  **Question:** What was the primary difference in the requirements for the third group in the 2001 British exercise study compared to the first two groups?
    *   **Answer:** While the first two groups tracked exercise or received motivational material, the third group was required to formulate a specific plan for when and where they would exercise. They completed a sentence specifying the day, time, and place of their intended 20-minute workout, resulting in a 91 percent success rate.
2.  **Question:** How do researchers define an "implementation intention"?
    *   **Answer:** An implementation intention is a plan made beforehand regarding exactly when and where an individual intends to act. It essentially pairs a specific situation with a predetermined response, following the format: "When situation X arises, I will perform response Y."
3.  **Question:** According to the text, why is "clarity" often more important than "motivation" when attempting to start a new habit?
    *   **Answer:** Many people fail to change habits not because they lack the will, but because they lack a concrete plan, leaving action up to chance or "inspiration." Clarity provides a specific instruction on how and when to act, removing the need to make a decision in the moment.
4.  **Question:** What are the two most common cues that trigger a habit?
    *   **Answer:** Time and location are identified as the two most common forms of cues that trigger a habit. Implementation intentions are specifically designed to leverage both of these triggers to make a habit more likely to occur.
5.  **Question:** What is the "Diderot Effect," and how does it relate to human behavior?
    *   **Answer:** The Diderot Effect is the tendency for obtaining a new possession to create a spiral of consumption that leads to additional purchases. In human behavior, this means no action happens in isolation; each behavior becomes a cue that triggers the next one in a chain reaction.
6.  **Question:** Explain the basic formula for "habit stacking."
    *   **Answer:** Habit stacking is a specific form of implementation intention that pairs a new habit with a current habit instead of a specific time and location. The formula is: "After [Current Habit], I will [New Habit]."
7.  **Question:** Why is it important to select a cue with the same frequency as the desired new habit when using habit stacking?
    *   **Answer:** If you want to perform a new habit every day, you must stack it on top of a current habit that also happens every day. Stacking a daily goal on a cue that only occurs once a week, such as on Mondays, will prevent the new habit from becoming a consistent daily routine.
8.  **Question:** How does having a specific implementation intention help a person resist distractions or derailments?
    *   **Answer:** Being specific about what you want and how you will achieve it makes it easier to say no to small requests that pull you off course. When goals are vague, it is easy to rationalize making "little exceptions" all day rather than performing the specific tasks needed for success.
9.  **Question:** What adjustment did the author make to his push-up habit stack to remove ambiguity?
    *   **Answer:** The author initially used the vague cue "When I take a break for lunch," which left it unclear when or where the push-ups would happen. He corrected this by making the trigger highly specific: "When I close my laptop for lunch, I will do ten push-ups next to my desk."
10. **Question:** What is the "1st Law of Behavior Change" as summarized in the text?
    *   **Answer:** The 1st Law of Behavior Change is to "make it obvious." Strategies like habit stacking and implementation intentions serve this law by creating clear, unavoidable cues that prompt action.

## Part 4: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **1st Law of Behavior Change** | The principle that to build a new habit, one must "make it obvious" by creating clear and visible cues. |
| **Cues** | Triggers that prompt a habit to begin; the two most common forms are time and location. |
| **Diderot Effect** | A phenomenon where obtaining a new possession (or performing an action) creates a spiral of consumption or behavior that leads to additional, related actions. |
| **Fresh Start** | A motivating period, such as the beginning of a week, month, or year, when people are more likely to take action because hope for improvement is higher. |
| **Habit Stacking** | A strategy for building new habits by identifying a current daily habit and "stacking" a new behavior on top of it. |
| **Implementation Intention** | A pre-determined plan that specifies exactly when and where a person will act, often formatted as: "I will [Behavior] at [Time] in [Location]." |
| **Motivation Group** | In the context of the 2001 study, the group that was informed of the benefits of a behavior (exercise) but did not create a specific plan for implementation. |
| **Specificity** | The quality of being clear and defined; in habit formation, it involves eliminating ambiguity regarding when, where, and how a behavior will occur. |
| **Tiny Habits** | A program created by BJ Fogg, the Stanford professor who originated the method of pairing new habits with existing ones. |
