# The Paper Clip Strategy: Why Visual Proof is the Secret to Unstoppable Habits

In 1993, a bank in Abbotsford, Canada, hired a twenty-three-year-old rookie stockbroker named Trent Dyrsmid. Abbotsford was a relatively small suburb, tucked away in the shadow of nearby Vancouver, where most of the big business deals were being made. Given his underdog status and his lack of experience, no one expected much from Dyrsmid. Yet, he made brisk progress by employing a system that was almost deceptively simple: two jars and 120 paper clips.

Every morning, Dyrsmid placed those two jars on his desk. One was filled with 120 paper clips; the other was empty. As soon as he settled in, he would make a sales call. Immediately after hanging up, he would move one clip from the full jar to the empty one. "I would keep dialing the phone until I had moved them all," he recalled. Within eighteen months, Dyrsmid was bringing in $5 million for his firm. By age twenty-four, he was earning $75,000 a year—the equivalent of roughly $125,000 today.

Most of us struggle with habits not because we lack willpower, but because we lack a sense of momentum. In the early stages of any routine, there is often a "valley of disappointment" where the results aren't yet visible. This is where most people quit. The Paper Clip Strategy succeeds because it transforms the invisible into the visible. It isn't just about data; it’s about the psychology of immediate satisfaction.

### The Visual Dopamine of the Paper Clip

The beauty of Dyrsmid’s strategy is its universal scalability. Whether you are shifting a hairpin for every page of a book you write or moving a marble for every set of push-ups, you are creating tangible evidence of your progress. This works because it aligns perfectly with the behavioral laws of change: it makes the habit obvious, attractive, and satisfying.

Visual cues act as triggers that initiate the next action. When you see a jar half-full of paper clips, you are reminded to act again. This feedback loop is remarkably effective; one study of over sixteen hundred people found that those who kept a daily food log lost twice as much weight as those who didn’t. The log itself became the motivation. Furthermore, visual measurement keeps you honest. It is easy to lie to yourself about how much effort you are putting in, but it is much harder to argue with a physical record.

To make this seamless, you can use the habit stacking formula found in Chapter 5 of the behavioral canon: **After [CURRENT HABIT], I will [TRACK MY HABIT].**
*   "After I hang up the phone from a sales call, I will move one paper clip over."
*   "After I finish each set at the gym, I will record it in my journal."

### The Geometry of Consistency: Never Break the Chain

History is filled with high-performers who obsessed over the "streak." Benjamin Franklin carried a booklet to track thirteen personal virtues, recording a mark for every lapse. Comedian Jerry Seinfeld used a wall calendar to track a "streak" of writing jokes every day. His only goal was to put a big red "X" over each day he worked.

Seinfeld’s genius was in his focus. He didn't fixate on how inspired he felt or the quality of the individual joke. He focused entirely on the process—on showing up. 

> "Don’t break the chain of sales calls and you’ll build a successful book of business. Don’t break the chain of workouts and you’ll get fit faster than you’d expect. Don’t break the chain of creating every day and you will end up with an impressive portfolio."

By prioritizing the streak over the result, you reduce the mental friction of high performance. Habit tracking provides visual proof that you are "casting votes" for the person you want to become. That immediate gratification is what sustains a habit until the long-term results arrive.

### The "Never Miss Twice" Recovery Rule

No matter how disciplined you are, life will eventually interrupt you. A sickness, a family emergency, or a travel delay is inevitable. Many people fall into an "all-or-nothing" cycle, where a single lapse feels like a failure, leading to total abandonment of the goal. 

To combat this, the most successful individuals follow the "Never Miss Twice" rule. Missing one day is an accident; missing twice is the start of a new habit. Reclaiming the routine quickly is more important than being perfect. There is a mathematical necessity to this recovery. Consider the math of compounding: if you start with $100, a 50 percent gain takes you to $150. However, you only need a 33 percent loss to take you back to $100. In other words, avoiding the loss is just as valuable as achieving the gain.

As Charlie Munger, vice chairman of Berkshire Hathaway, famously noted: “The first rule of compounding: Never interrupt it unnecessarily.” This is why "bad" workouts—the days where you only do ten squats or five minutes of cardio—are vital. They prevent the "zero" from eating into your compound gains and reaffirm your identity as someone who shows up, even when the performance isn't perfect.

### The Pitfall of Goodhart’s Law

While tracking is a powerful ally, it carries a hidden risk: becoming driven by the number rather than the purpose. This is Goodhart’s Law: "When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure." 

In our data-driven world, we often optimize for what is easy to quantify rather than what is actually important. We might focus on ten thousand steps while ignoring whether we are actually becoming healthier, or fixate on a lower number on the scale while ignoring the fact that our energy levels are crashing. 

It is vital to remember that just because something is easy to measure doesn't mean it’s the most important thing. To avoid this trap, look for "nonscale victories." If the number on the scale is stubborn, look for evidence that your skin is clearer, you’re waking up earlier, or your focus has improved. Numbers should be feedback for the system, not a consuming obsession.

### Beyond the Numbers: Identity Reinforcement

Ultimately, habit tracking is not about the data. It is about identity reinforcement. Every time you move a paper clip, you are providing yourself with immediate, intrinsic gratification. You are confirming that you are becoming the type of person who does the work. 

The goal of a habit tracker is to keep your eye on the ball: the process, not the result. If you find yourself struggling to stay consistent, stop looking at the distant horizon. Instead, focus on keeping the chain alive for today. 

If you stopped focusing on the end result today and focused only on keeping the chain alive, where would you be in eighteen months?
