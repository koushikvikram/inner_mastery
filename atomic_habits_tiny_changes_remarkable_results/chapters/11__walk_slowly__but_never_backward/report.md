# Why Your Quest for Perfection is Keeping You From Success: The Power of "Putting in Your Reps"

### **1. Introduction: The Trap of the Optimal Plan**

It is a remarkably common cycle: you decide to lose weight, build muscle, or launch a side business, and you immediately dive into an exhaustive research phase. You spend weeks hunting for the fastest fat-loss protocol, the perfect five-day workout split, or the "million-dollar" niche. You are searching for the optimal plan before you take a single step.

In behavioral science, we often see this manifest as **analysis paralysis**—a cognitive bias where the fear of making a "sub-optimal" choice prevents any choice from being made at all. While this feels like progress, this quest for the "best" approach is actually your biggest obstacle. We become so focused on figuring out the perfect path that we never actually get around to walking it. To achieve real change, we must realize that the most effective form of learning is not planning, but practice.

### **2. Takeaway 1: The Surprising Link Between Quantity and Quality**

To understand why perfectionism fails, consider an experiment conducted by Jerry Uelsmann, a professor at the University of Florida. On the first day of his film photography class, he divided his students into two groups.

The "quantity" group would be graded solely on the amount of work they produced. One hundred photos earned an A, ninety earned a B, and so on. The "quality" group, however, would be graded only on the excellence of their work. They needed to produce only one photo during the semester, but to get an A, it had to be a nearly perfect image.

At the end of the term, Uelsmann discovered a counter-intuitive result: all the best photos were produced by the quantity group. 

While the quality group sat around "speculating about perfection" and ended the semester with nothing to show for their efforts but unverified theories and **one mediocre photo**, the quantity group was busy taking action. They experimented with composition, tested darkroom methods, and learned from their mistakes. By focusing on volume, they naturally honed the skills required to create high-quality work. As the source notes:

> “The best is the enemy of the good.” — Voltaire

### **3. Takeaway 2: Are You Taking Action or Just Moving?**

The reason we often fail to produce results is that we confuse "being in motion" with "taking action." While these concepts sound similar, they serve very different psychological functions.

*   **Motion** involves planning, strategizing, and learning. These are useful for preparation, but they do not deliver an outcome on their own.
*   **Action** is the type of behavior that directly produces a result.

To illustrate the difference:
*   **Motion:** Outlining twenty ideas for articles. **Action:** Actually writing an article.
*   **Motion:** Searching for a better diet plan and reading books on nutrition. **Action:** Eating a healthy meal.
*   **Motion:** Talking to a personal trainer. **Action:** Completing a workout.

We often slip into motion because it is **deceptive**. It allows us to feel a "pseudo-achievement" dopamine hit, making us believe we are making progress without ever running the risk of failure. Motion acts as a psychological safety net; most of us are experts at avoiding criticism or public judgment. By staying in the planning phase, you successfully delay the possibility of failing. To master a habit, you must step out of the safety of motion and start getting your "reps" in.

### **4. Takeaway 3: Habits Form Based on Frequency, Not Time**

A common question in productivity circles is, "How long does it take to form a new habit?" You have likely heard the **common myth** that it takes exactly 21 days. In reality, there is nothing magical about the mere passage of time.

Habit formation is a process called **automaticity**—the ability to perform a behavior without thinking about each step. This occurs when the nonconscious mind takes over. Whether it takes 21 days or 300 days is irrelevant; what matters is the *frequency* of the behavior. 

In the beginning (Point A), a habit requires significant effort and conscious attention. As you repeat the behavior (Point B), it moves toward the "**Habit Line**." Once you cross this threshold (Point C), the behavior is firmly embedded. Doing something twice in thirty days is not the same as doing it two hundred times in thirty days. It is the number of repetitions that allows you to cross the Habit Line and achieve true automaticity.

### **5. Takeaway 4: Your Brain Literally Rewires Itself Through Repetition**

The power of repetition is not just a psychological concept; it has a biological basis. "Automaticity" is essentially the behavioral manifestation of a neurological process called **long-term potentiation**.

This process is governed by **Hebb’s Law**: "Neurons that fire together wire together." Every time you repeat an action, you activate a specific neural circuit, and the cell-to-cell signaling between those neurons improves and tightens. This leads to clear physical changes in the brain:

*   **Musicians:** Often have a larger cerebellum, which is critical for physical movements like plucking strings.
*   **Mathematicians:** Show increased gray matter in the inferior parietal lobule. Crucially, the size of this gray matter is **directly correlated with the amount of time spent in the field**, proving that the change is a result of cumulative effort.
*   **London Taxi Drivers:** Possess a significantly larger hippocampus (involved in spatial memory) than non-drivers, though this region atrophies if they retire and stop practicing.

Just as muscles grow through weight training, brain regions adapt through use. As the English philosopher George H. Lewes observed:

> “In learning to speak a new language, to play on a musical instrument, or to perform unaccustomed movements, great difficulty is felt, because the channels through which each sensation has to pass have not become established; but no sooner has frequent repetition cut a pathway, than this difficulty vanishes.”

### **6. Conclusion: From Planning to Practicing**

If you want to master a habit, you must prioritize action over optimal planning. Focus on the frequency of your attempts rather than the perfection of your results. 

The most effective way to ensure this practice happens is to adhere to the **3rd Law of Behavior Change: make it easy.** By making the action easy, you lower the cognitive load required to perform the first rep. This is the only way to facilitate the frequency needed to rewire your brain and eventually reach the state of automaticity.

**Ask yourself:** What is one "motion" you’ve been hiding behind to delay failure, and what is the smallest "action" you can take today to replace it?
