# Study Guide: Making Good Habits Inevitable and Bad Habits Impossible

This study guide provides a comprehensive review of the strategies used to lock in future behavior by making good habits inevitable and bad habits impossible. The content is based on the principles of the 3rd Law of Behavior Change and the utilization of commitment devices and automation.

## Answer Key

1.  **Victor Hugo’s Strategy:** Hugo overcame procrastination by having an assistant lock all his clothes in a chest, leaving him with only a large shawl to wear. Because he lacked suitable clothing to go outdoors, he was forced to stay in his study and focus entirely on writing, eventually finishing his book two weeks early.
2.  **Definition of a Commitment Device:** A commitment device is a choice made in the present that controls and locks in future actions. Its purpose is to bind an individual to good habits and restrict them from bad ones by making temptation more difficult to act upon.
3.  **The Inversion of the 3rd Law:** The 3rd Law of Behavior Change states that one should "make it easy" to build good habits; therefore, its inversion is to "make it difficult" for bad habits. This approach focuses on increasing friction until a bad habit becomes impractical or impossible to perform.
4.  **Individual Packaging as a Device:** By purchasing food in individual portions rather than bulk, a person creates a commitment device that reduces overeating. This setup forces the individual to make a conscious effort to open another package, adding a layer of friction to the habit of eating.
5.  **The Incorruptible Cashier:** This was the first cash register, designed to automatically lock cash and receipts inside after every transaction to prevent employee theft. After John Henry Patterson installed two of these machines, theft vanished and his business moved from a loss to a significant profit.
6.  **Onetime Actions:** Onetime actions are single choices—like buying a better mattress or unsubscribing from emails—that require a small amount of effort upfront but deliver continuous returns over time. These actions automate future habits and improve quality of life without requiring daily willpower.
7.  **Technology and Infrequent Tasks:** For behaviors that happen too rarely to become habitual, technology can "remember" the task on behalf of the individual. By using tools like automatic refills for prescriptions or automatic wage deductions for retirement, the right behavior is guaranteed even without a recurring habit.
8.  **The Downside of Automation:** Automation can create a level of convenience that allows individuals to act on every small whim or impulse, such as binge-watching or social media scrolling. This can lead to jumping from one easy, low-reward task to another without making time for more difficult, rewarding work.
9.  **Author’s Social Media Strategy:** The author had his assistant reset his social media passwords every Monday morning, effectively locking him out of his accounts for the duration of the work week. On Friday, the assistant would provide the new passwords so he could enjoy social media over the weekend before the cycle repeated.
10. **Whitehead’s View on Civilization:** Whitehead argued that civilization advances by increasing the number of operations and tasks that people can perform without having to think about them. By automating basic or repetitive actions, individuals free up time and energy to focus on the next stage of growth and more complex challenges.

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **3rd Law of Behavior Change** | A principle of habit formation that suggests making a desired behavior easy to perform to increase its frequency. |
| **Automation** | The use of technology or systems to perform tasks automatically, ensuring a behavior occurs without the need for conscious effort or recurring decision-making. |
| **Commitment Device** | A choice made in the present that restricts future choices and locks in better behavior by making bad habits difficult and good habits easier. |
| **Friction** | The degree of difficulty or the number of steps required to perform an action; increasing friction makes a habit harder to do, while decreasing it makes it easier. |
| **Incorruptible Cashier** | The first cash register, invented by James Ritty, which automated ethical behavior by making it practically impossible for employees to steal. |
| **Inversion of the 3rd Law** | The strategy of breaking bad habits by making them difficult, impractical, or impossible to perform. |
| **Onetime Action** | A single decision or purchase that automates a future habit and provides ongoing benefits with no additional effort required (e.g., setting up automatic bill pay). |
| **Outlet Timer** | A hardware device used as a commitment device to automatically cut power to a router at a specific time, forcing a sleep habit. |
| **Two-Minute Rule** | A strategy (referenced in the cheat sheet) to downscale habits until they can be performed in two minutes or less, making them easier to start. |
| **Website Blocker** | A technological tool used to automate productivity by preventing access to social media or distracting sites during work hours. |
