## Answer Key

1. **What is the definition of an "atomic habit"?**
An atomic habit is a regular practice or routine that is not only small and easy to do but also a source of incredible power. It represents a tiny change, a marginal gain, or a 1 percent improvement that serves as a building block for a larger system of remarkable results.

2. **Explain the mathematical impact of getting 1 percent better every day for a year.**
If you improve by 1 percent each day for one year, the effects compound to make you thirty-seven times better by the time you are done. Conversely, if you get 1 percent worse every day for a year, your habits will decline nearly down to zero.

3. **What is the "Plateau of Latent Potential," and why can it be discouraging?**
The Plateau of Latent Potential is the critical threshold where a habit must persist long enough to break through a level of performance where results were previously "stored" but not visible. It is discouraging because people often expect progress to be linear and may give up in the "Valley of Disappointment" when they fail to see immediate results from their efforts.

4. **Contrast the difference between "goals" and "systems" as defined in the text.**
Goals are focused on the specific results you want to achieve, such as winning a championship or losing weight. Systems are about the processes and routines that lead to those results, such as the way a coach recruits players or how an individual manages their daily diet.

5. **What are the three layers of behavior change, and which layer is the most effective for long-term change?**
The three layers are outcomes (changing your results), processes (changing your habits and systems), and identity (changing your beliefs and worldview). The most effective way to build lasting habits is to focus on changing the deepest layer: your identity.

6. **How do identity-based habits differ from outcome-based habits?**
Outcome-based habits focus on what you want to achieve, while identity-based habits focus on who you wish to become. For example, rather than having a goal to "read a book," an identity-based approach focuses on "becoming a reader."

7. **Identify and briefly define the four stages of the habit loop.**
The four stages are the **cue** (the trigger that predicts a reward), the **craving** (the motivational force or desire to change your internal state), the **response** (the actual habit or action performed), and the **reward** (the end goal that satisfies the craving and teaches the brain which actions are worth repeating).

8. **What was the primary conclusion of Edward Thorndike’s puzzle box experiment with cats?**
Thorndike concluded that behaviors followed by satisfying consequences tend to be repeated, while those that produce unpleasant consequences are less likely to be repeated. This trial-and-error process is the foundation of how habits are formed in the brain.

9. **How does the "Pointing-and-Calling" system improve safety and awareness?**
The system requires operators to physically point at and verbally name various signals and actions, which raises the level of awareness from a nonconscious habit to a conscious level. By involving the eyes, hands, mouth, and ears, it makes it much more likely that the individual will notice problems before they occur.

10. **Explain the formula for "habit stacking."**
Habit stacking is a strategy where you identify a current habit you already perform daily and then "stack" a new behavior on top of it. The specific formula is: "After [Current Habit], I will [New Habit]."

## Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Atomic Habit** | A tiny change, a marginal gain, or a 1 percent improvement that is a component of a larger system of compound growth. |
| **Aggregation of Marginal Gains** | The philosophy of searching for a tiny margin of improvement in everything you do; the cumulative effect of many small improvements. |
| **Compound Interest of Self-Improvement** | The phenomenon where the effects of habits multiply as you repeat them over months and years. |
| **Craving** | The second step of the habit loop; the motivational force or desire to change your internal state. |
| **Cue** | The first step of the habit loop; a bit of information that triggers the brain to initiate a behavior by predicting a reward. |
| **Diderot Effect** | The tendency for obtaining a new possession to create a spiral of consumption that leads to additional purchases. |
| **Habit** | A routine or practice performed regularly; an automatic response to a specific situation. |
| **Habit Stacking** | A strategy that pairs a new habit with a current habit to create an obvious cue for the new behavior. |
| **Habits Scorecard** | A simple exercise used to become more aware of behavior by listing daily habits and categorizing them as good, bad, or neutral. |
| **Identity-Based Habits** | Habits that focus on the type of person one wishes to become rather than the results one wants to achieve. |
| **Implementation Intention** | A plan made beforehand about when and where to act, usually following the format: "When situation X arises, I will perform response Y." |
| **Plateau of Latent Potential** | The period during which a habit seems to make no difference because the results are being "stored" rather than manifesting immediately. |
| **Pointing-and-Calling** | A safety system that reduces errors by raising the level of awareness through physical pointing and verbal naming of actions. |
| **Response** | The third step of the habit loop; the actual habit or thought performed. |
| **Reward** | The fourth step of the habit loop; the end goal of every habit that satisfies the craving and teaches the brain which actions to remember. |
| **Valley of Disappointment** | The initial phase of a compounding process where progress is not yet visible, leading to feelings of discouragement. |
