# The Art of Strategy: A Synthesis of Sun Tzu, Boyd, and Wardley for Business Agility

## Executive Summary

The provided text, "The Art of Strategy," interpreted and compiled by Erik Schön, synthesizes the timeless wisdom of Sun Tzu with the modern strategic frameworks of John Boyd (the OODA loop) and Simon Wardley (Wardley Mapping). This briefing document outlines a modern approach to strategy, shifting from a focus on combat and power toward a paradigm of organizational purpose and sustainability in a volatile, uncertain, complex, and ambiguous (VUCA) world.

The core of this synthesis is the **Strategy Cycle**, which harmonizes Sun Tzu’s five factors with Boyd's OODA loop and Wardley's environmental mapping. Strategy is defined not as a static plan, but as a dynamic "mental tapestry of changing intentions" (Boyd) and the "art of manipulating an environment to gain a desirable outcome" (Wardley). 

Critical takeaways include:
*   **Situational Awareness:** Success depends on a deep understanding of the landscape (value chains) and climate (economic patterns).
*   **The Primacy of Orientation:** Orientation is the most critical part of the OODA loop, shaping how an organization observes, decides, and acts based on its "genetic heritage" and "cultural traditions."
*   **Doctrine over Dogma:** Organizations must adopt universally applicable principles (Doctrine) while remaining flexible enough to destroy and recreate them as the environment evolves.
*   **Agility and Momentum:** Agility is defined as the ability to adapt to and influence situations more skillfully than the competition, achieved through a combination of the "surprising" and the "expected."

## I. Definitions and Conceptual Framework

The document provides three distinct but overlapping definitions of strategy from its core thinkers:

| Thinker | Definition of Strategy |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Sun Tzu** | The art of manipulating an environment to gain a desirable outcome. |
| **John Boyd** | A mental tapestry of changing intentions for harmonizing and focusing efforts as a basis for realizing some aim or purpose in an unfolding and often unforeseen world. |
| **Simon Wardley** | A game of position and movement played using the strategy cycle; the art of manipulating an environment to gain a desirable outcome through awareness and toolsets. |

### The Five Fundamental Factors (Sun Tzu)
Sun Tzu identifies five factors that must be considered when strategizing:
1.  **Purpose:** Keeps people united and supporting each other through success and failure.
2.  **Landscape:** The environment, including positions, distances, and obstacles.
3.  **Climate:** The forces acting on the environment (seasons, stakeholder actions).
4.  **Doctrine:** Ways of operating and communicating that apply regardless of landscape or climate.
5.  **Leadership:** Actions and decisions guided by wisdom, trust, compassion, courage, and strictness.

## II. The Strategy Cycle: Observe, Orient, Decide, Act

The text integrates Sun Tzu’s factors into Boyd’s **OODA loop**, creating a continuous cycle for organizational response.

### Observation (Gathering Data and Facts)
*   Observation includes gathering information from the environment and outside sources.
*   It is the starting point for situational awareness, anchored by stakeholder needs.

### Orientation (The Engine of Strategy)
*   Boyd considers **Orientation** the most important part of the loop. It is a repository of genetic heritage, cultural tradition, and previous experiences.
*   It is a process of "many-sided implicit cross-referencing" (projections, empathies, correlations, and rejections).
*   Without a many-sided orientation, organizations cannot perform the analysis and synthesis necessary to generate novelty or address mismatches between their mental models and reality.

### Decision and Action
*   **Decision:** Shaping and choosing favorable conditions.
*   **Act:** Testing hypotheses through action, then repeating the cycle. 
*   **The Two Whys:** Organizations must distinguish between the "Why of purpose" (the goal, like winning a game) and the "Why of movement" (positioning, like moving a queen in chess).

## III. Wardley Mapping: Visualizing the Landscape

Wardley Mapping is a tool used to observe the game by looking at the landscape and climatic patterns.

### The Evolution Axis
Components on a map evolve from left to right across four stages:
1.  **Genesis:** Unique, rare, uncertain, and constantly changing. Focus is on exploration.
2.  **Custom Built:** Very uncommon, tailored, and learning-focused.
3.  **Product (+ rental):** Increasingly common, repeatable processes, becoming stable and defined.
4.  **Commodity (+ utility):** Scale and volume operations, highly standardized, undifferentiated, and focused on operational efficiency.

### Climatic Patterns
These are economic patterns that affect all players and help anticipate change:
*   **Everything Evolves:** Components move toward industrialization due to supply and demand competition.
*   **Success Breeds Inertia:** Past success creates resistance to change because of a fear of losing physical, social, financial, or political capital.
*   **Characteristics Change:** The skills needed for the "uncharted" space (Genesis) are the polar opposite of those needed for "industrialized" spaces (Commodity).

## IV. Doctrine: Universal Principles for Success

Doctrine provides a "smorgasbord" of choices to improve an organization’s situation. 

### Key Doctrinal Principles:
*   **Focus on User Needs:** The user need is the "anchor" for any map. Organizations should aim to fulfill their own needs by meeting the needs of their users.
*   **Common Language:** Maps provide a visual, context-specific language that allows diverse groups (finance, IT, marketing) to collaborate effectively.
*   **Think Small:** Large systems should be broken into small components. Use small teams (e.g., Amazon’s "Two Pizza" model) to provide autonomy and mastery.
*   **Be Transparent:** Organizations should have a bias toward openness, allowing maps to be challenged and refined by others.
*   **Remove Duplication:** Use maps to find where teams are custom-building components that already exist as commodities or are being built elsewhere in the organization.

### Organizational Attitudes
Organizations need three distinct "attitudes" to manage components at different stages of evolution:
*   **Explorers (Pioneers):** Thrive on chaos and "crazy" ideas; they enable future success.
*   **Villagers (Settlers):** Turn prototypes into profitable products and manufacture trust.
*   **Town Planners:** Industrialize products, making them faster, cheaper, and more efficient through economies of scale.

## V. Strategic Execution: Momentum and Shaping

### Generating Momentum
Momentum arises from the combination of the **surprising** and the **expected**. 
*   Skilled leadership uses the expected to engage and the surprise to succeed.
*   Momentum is compared to "rolling rocks down a ten-thousand-foot mountain"—a force so massive and timing so tight it becomes unstoppable.

### Shaping the Environment
*   Shaping involves making the organization resilient while waiting for the competition’s fragility.
*   **Formlessness:** A key strategy is to remain "formless" or "soundless." If the competition cannot see your setup, they do not know where to attack or defend, forcing them to spread their resources thin.
*   **Strength against Weakness:** Always avoid the competition's strength and rush to their weakness, much like water avoids high ground and rushes to the low.

### Engagement Rules
*   **Mastering Spirit:** Avoid competitors when their spirit is high; move when it is drained.
*   **Mastering Emotions:** Wait for disorder with discipline and for commotion with calmness.
*   **The "Golden Bridge":** When a competitor is surrounded, leave an outlet; do not press a cornered foe to the point where they fight with desperate energy.

## VI. Resilience and Sustainability

The document distinguishes between two types of resilience for long-term success:
*   **Engineering Resilience:** Focused on maintaining the efficiency of a function (robustness). Many large organizations fall into this category but are ill-prepared for rapid change.
*   **Ecological Resilience:** Focused on maintaining the *existence* of a function. This requires a structure that adapts to constant evolution and embraces diversity of culture and thought.

**Final Conclusion:** To succeed, an organization must secure harmony among stakeholders, keep competition off balance, and evolve superior capabilities to influence, adapt, and map the unfolding world. Success is determined before the engagement even starts through careful assessment and the creation of advantageous conditions.
