# Study Guide: Changing Your Story by Bill Beswick

This study guide provides a comprehensive review of the principles of performance psychology as outlined in Bill Beswick’s *Changing Your Story*. The document is structured into a short-answer quiz, an answer key, suggested essay topics, and a glossary of key terms.

## Part 2: Answer Key

1. **Mindset as the Driver:** This phrase signifies that mental preparation and thinking patterns are the primary factors in releasing an individual’s physical potential. While the body executes the actions, the mind is responsible for the energy, discipline, and emotional control required for peak performance.
2. **Taking Responsibility:** "Crossing the Line" represents the choice to take full responsibility for one's situation and destiny rather than letting circumstances dictate life. It involves a commitment to stop making excuses or blaming others and to accept accountability for one's actions and responses to failure.
3. **Foundational Motivation:** The three questions are: "What do you want?", "How badly do you want it?", and "How much are you willing to suffer?" These answers form the basis of an athlete's passion and commitment, helping them connect their desired outcome with a realistic plan to endure the necessary hardships.
4. **Expectations Driving Reality:** A self-fulfilling prophecy occurs when an individual’s behavior follows their expectations; if an athlete believes they are inferior to an opponent based on rankings, they often "choke" or perform poorly. Their internal narrative creates a behavioral norm that limits their actual capability.
5. **Color-Coded Control:** This strategy uses colors to track emotional states: "Green" means being in control and focused; "Amber" is a warning sign of losing control; and "Red" is a non-productive state of emotional overreaction. Athletes use trigger phrases to recognize "Amber" moments and snap back to "Green" to maintain discipline.
6. **Realistic Standards vs. Rigid Goals:** Perfectionism is a way of thinking where anything less than 100% success is viewed as a catastrophe, often leading to guilt and shame. Mastery, by contrast, is the intense desire to achieve while accepting that mistakes are valuable learning opportunities, focusing on "raising the bottom line" rather than chasing an unattainable ideal.
7. **Incremental Progress:** A "Tick Stepladder" is a plan consisting of small, doable daily actions that lead toward a long-term goal. It is effective because it builds achievement momentum and sustainable habits, making significant change more achievable through a series of manageable successes.
8. **Capacity Management:** Personal bandwidth refers to the number of situations an individual can handle before losing focus or feeling overwhelmed. Because high performance is a series of "sprints" rather than a marathon, recovery time is necessary to replenish the energy required for the next peak effort.
9. **Physical/Emotional Checklist:** HATED stands for Hungry, Angry (or Alcohol), Tired, Emotional, and Dehydrated. It is used as a self-check tool to identify underlying conditions that might increase an athlete's susceptibility to losing emotional control.
10. **Decluttering the Mind:** Journaling helps transfer cluttered, ruminating thoughts onto paper, providing emotional relief and clarity. By answering specific questions about what went well and how to improve, an athlete can challenge negative perceptions with factual evidence of their progress.

## Part 4: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **A Grade Life** | A life characterized by a high level of commitment to maximize resources and take full advantage of every opportunity. |
| **Belief Partner** | A trusted person (family or friend) who constantly motivates you, stresses the positives, and intervenes when negative self-talk creeps in. |
| **Controllables** | Factors within an individual's power, such as effort, attitude, and preparation; focusing here reduces anxiety. |
| **Defining the Moment** | Taking responsibility and acting decisively during critical, high-pressure situations rather than being paralyzed by them. |
| **FAMILY** | An acronym for "Forget About Me I Love You," representing the concept of total mutual support within a team. |
| **Fighter Mentality** | A mindset characterized by taking responsibility, staying positive, and viewing difficulties as challenges to be overcome. |
| **HATED** | A checklist to monitor susceptibility to losing control: Hungry, Angry, Tired, Emotional, Dehydrated. |
| **Mastery** | The pursuit of excellence that accepts mistakes as part of the process, aiming for the best possible performance without the frustration of perfectionism. |
| **Personal Bandwidth** | The mental and emotional capacity to handle life's various demands before becoming overwhelmed. |
| **Picture of Perfection** | A visualization technique where an individual imagines the best possible successful outcome of a challenge. |
| **Raising the Bottom Line** | A strategy focusing on improving one’s "worst" performance (e.g., from 60% to 90%) to ensure consistency, rather than only chasing the "top line" of perfection. |
| **SOCS** | A problem-solving acronym for anxiety management: Situation, Options, Consequences, Solution. |
| **Thinking Partner** | A mentor or neutral friend who helps an individual "get off the treadmill" to see the big picture and process emotions rationally. |
| **Trigger Card** | A physical card with phrases describing an individual at their best, used to flip the mindset from negative to positive. |
| **Uncontrollables** | External factors an individual cannot change, such as weather, crowds, or the opinions of others; dwelling here leads to "red mist" or panic. |
| **Victim Mentality** | A mindset characterized by making excuses, blaming others, and hiding from pressure. |
