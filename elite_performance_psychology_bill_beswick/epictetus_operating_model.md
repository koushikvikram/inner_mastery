# Epictetus Operating Model — Bill Beswick, *Elite Performance Psychology*


![Epictetus Operating Model — Bill Beswick, *Elite Performance Psychology*](./epictetus_operating_model.png)

> Source frame: high performance is the **battle of you versus you** — every morning a binary choice between the **fighter** (takes responsibility, faces challenge) and the **victim** (makes excuses, blames environment). Greatness requires crossing the **line of responsibility** from passenger to pilot, and developing a **Performer Self** (the assertive persona you project at high stakes) distinct from your **Real Self**. Talent carries you through Steps 1–7 of the **10 Steps to Greatness**; at **Step 7 — the moment of truth — character takes over** ("enough talent plus great character"). "Genetics deals the cards, but environment plays the hand," and **attitude is a choice** (an "A for Attitude" can beat B-level talent). The work is to **love the process** over the outcome, **raise your bottom line** of consistency rather than chase perfection, and answer three brutal questions: what do you want, how badly, and how much will you suffer.

## Layer 1 — Practice (What must be done?)

### What we’re trying to achieve
- Win the daily "you versus you" battle by choosing the fighter over the victim.
- Cross and stay above the **line of responsibility** — own your story, lead your own life.
- Raise the **bottom line** (worst-day floor) so performance becomes reliable and trusted.
- Fall in love with the process so the skills (commitment, selflessness, resilience) carry into all of life.

### Non-negotiable behaviors
- **Choose fighter, daily**: decide to be "at the game" regardless of obstacles; "don't let the old man in."
- **Answer the three questions honestly**: What do you want? How badly? How much will you suffer?
- **Operate above the line**: take the lead role; act, don't wait to be told.
- **Project the Performer Self**: inject the assertiveness the moment demands, even if the Real Self is shy or tired.
- **Forgive mistakes, stay in the game**: protect the 85% floor instead of chasing a flawless 100%.
- **Train to dominate**: treat every session as making victory inevitable, not merely possible.

### Routines / checklists

- **Morning fighter check-in (2–3 min)**
  - Name the obstacle in front of you today (fatigue, fear, setback).
  - Decide explicitly: *fighter or victim?*
  - Set the day's identity: "the best [version of me] there is today."

- **The Three Questions diagnostic (before any big commitment)**
  1. **What do you want?** — define the outcome and your life story.
  2. **How badly do you want it?** — name the depth of commitment.
  3. **How much are you willing to suffer?** — accept suffering as the daily currency of the podium.

- **Attitude ladder audit (where am I training today?)**
  1. Just turn up. → 2. Turn up to train. → 3. Turn up to compete. → 4. **Train to win.** → 5. **Train to dominate.**
  - Target level 4–5; "win the race in the dark, while no one is watching" (the Adam Peaty rhythm).

- **Raise-the-bottom-line protocol (the 85% Rule)**
  - Set the target as a consistent 85%, not a perfect 100%.
  - Forgive the error immediately; stay in the game and keep contributing.
  - Build trust: teammates must know exactly what to expect from you every day.
  - (Stakes adjust the floor: a fire-and-rescue context demands 10/10 — an off day is not an option.)

- **21-Day Small-Steps Turnaround (for discipline / habit collapse)**
  - Assign one small professional behavior per day (arrive early, look the part, shake hands and make eye contact, be first on and last off).
  - Let how others perceive you reshape how you perceive yourself.
  - By Day 21 the bottom line has risen through accumulated consistency.

- **Journaling release (evening, raging emotions)**
  - Left page: write down the fears and anxieties.
  - Right page: write what can be done about each tomorrow.
  - Move from an emotional state to a rational, coping-oriented state.

- **Kobe resilience reset (after a setback)**
  - Work *beyond the emotion* of the miss to find the logic.
  - Analyze the mechanics of the failure rather than wallowing.
  - Convert the setback into a coping strategy so it won't recur.

### What good execution looks like
- You can state today's fighter choice and the obstacle you chose to face.
- Your worst performances are getting better — the floor is rising, not just the ceiling.
- You behave like a professional in the small things (punctual, presentable, present).
- Setbacks produce analysis and an action plan, not excuses or self-pity.
- You love the process for its own rewards, treating the medal as "the cake at the end of the meal."

## Layer 2 — Principles (Why does it work?)

- **You versus you**: the real opponent is internal doubt, fear, and excuse-making — manage that and you maximize the self.
- **Character dominates at Step 7**: once everyone is talented, only character separates the great from the merely gifted.
- **Attitude is chosen, talent is given**: "genetics deals the cards, but environment plays the hand"; A-attitude beats B-talent.
- **The bottom line wins seasons**: consistency and trust matter more than occasional perfection.
- **Suffering is the price, not the obstacle**: gold-medal commitment means embracing the pain barrier daily.
- **Life begins at the edge of the comfort zone**: comfort breeds mental entropy; challenge builds resourcefulness.
- **Truth-telling stretches people**: a coach is a "stretch," not a "shrink" — candor and reframed limits drive growth.
- **Vulnerability is strength**: "I need help" / "I got that wrong" is the mechanism of self-optimization.

### Decision rules (doctrine)
- If you catch yourself making an excuse, you've slipped below the line — choose fighter and step back over it.
- If you're chasing a perfect 100%, switch to defending an honest 85% and stay in the game.
- If your Real Self feels lousy, deploy the Performer Self anyway — the audience cares only how you perform.
- If discipline has collapsed, don't overhaul everything; assign one small professional behavior per day.
- If emotion is hijacking you after a setback, journal it out (fears left, actions right) and find the logic.
- If you can't answer "how much will you suffer," you haven't truly committed to the goal yet.

## Layer 3 — Meta (How do we evaluate and improve?)

### Metrics (leading, character-tied)
- **Fighter-day ratio** (days you chose fighter over victim, weekly).
- **Bottom-line trend** (your worst performance level, tracked over time).
- **Attitude-ladder position** (which of the 5 training levels you operated at).
- **Small-steps streak** (consecutive days of professional behaviors held).
- **Recovery latency** (time from setback to logical, action-oriented response).
- **Process-love index** (engagement with daily work independent of results).

### Review loop
- **Daily**: morning fighter check-in; evening journal (fears left, actions right).
- **Weekly**: review the bottom-line trend and attitude-ladder position; recommit to the three questions.
- **Monthly**: character audit — where am I on the 10 Steps, and is character (not just talent) carrying me past Step 7?

### Core rule guardrail
- If you are obsessing over the outcome (the medal, the profit, the accolade) and neglecting the daily process, you've inverted the model — return to loving the process and raising the bottom line. No amount of metrics or mental "tricks" substitutes for the morning decision to be a fighter. If your story is being written by someone else's lines, take the pen back: take a mental timeout, reframe, and rewrite from this moment.

---

## Operating Sequence

1. Choose the fighter and step over the line of responsibility.
2. Answer the three questions: what, how badly, how much suffering.
3. Train to dominate while raising your bottom line (85% floor).
4. Deploy the Performer Self under pressure; journal and reset after setbacks.
5. Review fighter-days and bottom-line trend; recommit to the process.
6. Repeat until character — not talent — is what carries you.
