# Epictetus Operating Model — Atul Gawande, *Better*

> Source frame: better is not a heroic leap; it is a relentless attention to **the particulars**. The book’s three pillars are **Diligence** (the unglamorous discipline of doing the basics perfectly, repeatedly), **Doing Right** (ethical conduct under pressure and ambiguity), and **Ingenuity** (creative problem-solving inside real constraints). The hallmark behaviors of a "**positive deviant**" — the practitioner who outperforms the system — are: **ask an unscripted question, don’t complain, count something, write something, embrace change**. Excellence is the willingness to *return to the bedside* one more time when standard procedure says you’re done.

## Layer 1 — Practice (What must be done?)

### What we’re trying to achieve
- Move from "competent" (mastery of rules) to "excellent" (obsessive attention to particulars).
- Build a daily diligence floor that catches the things others miss.
- Make ethical choices under pressure without flinching.
- Use ingenuity inside constraints — not as a reason to wait for better tools.
- Become a positive deviant: someone who outperforms the system from inside it.

### Non-negotiable behaviors
- **Return to the bedside one more time** — the extra check is the difference.
- **Sweat the basics** (hand-washing-tier rituals): boring discipline beats clever heroics.
- **Count something** every week — measurement is the door to improvement.
- **Write something** — make the unseen visible to others.
- **Ask one unscripted question** per day to break out of the script.
- **Don’t complain** — the energy spent complaining is unavailable for fixing.

### Routines / checklists

- **Daily diligence floor (5–15 min)**
  - List the boring basics for your role (the "hand-washing" of your craft).
  - Run them every day — no exceptions, no improvisation.
  - End-of-day: confirm each was done.

- **One return-to-the-bedside (daily)**
  - Identify one thing you’d normally consider "handled."
  - Go back, look once more, ask one more question.
  - Log what changed (often: nothing; occasionally: everything).

- **Count something (weekly, 10–15 min)**
  - Pick a small, specific metric inside your work (not a vanity number).
  - Track it for 4 weeks before drawing conclusions.
  - Examples: response latency, error type X, dropped requests, undocumented decisions.

- **Write something (weekly, 30–60 min)**
  - Write a short note, memo, or post-mortem about something you learned.
  - Force yourself into clarity by writing it down.
  - Share at least one piece with a peer per month.

- **Unscripted question (daily, in conversations)**
  - Ask one question you haven’t asked before.
  - Examples: *"What’s the smallest thing that bothers you about this?" "Why can’t you do X?" (instead of "Why don’t you?")*
  - Listen for what isn’t in the script.

- **Positive deviance scan (monthly)**
  - Find the person/team in your environment quietly outperforming.
  - Study their *particulars* — not their slogans.
  - Steal one specific behavior and install it next week.

- **Doing Right protocol (in ethical gray zones)**
  - Name the conflict explicitly (head, not gut).
  - Identify what the role demands and what the person needs.
  - Choose the action you can defend out loud, not just inside.

### What good execution looks like
- The boring basics are unmistakably done — every day, no improvisation.
- "One more check" produces small interventions that quietly prevent disasters.
- You have at least one number you’re tracking inside your work right now.
- You can point to something you wrote this week that wasn’t for performance.
- The script in meetings is broken by your unscripted question.
- Complaints are replaced by interventions.

## Layer 2 — Principles (Why does it work?)

- **Excellence is in the particulars**: the gap between competence and excellence is the small acts of conscientiousness no rule mandates.
- **Diligence is the silent virtue**: most failures aren’t knowledge gaps; they’re routine gaps.
- **Data is a survival weapon**: feedback loops (battlefield trauma logs, MRSA tracking) save lives where rhetoric does not.
- **Positive deviance beats expert dogma**: the answers are often inside the system, in the people quietly succeeding.
- **Ingenuity is constrained creativity**: don’t wait for new tools; use what you have, better.
- **Ethics is operational**: doing right under pressure is a practice, not a personality.

### Decision rules (doctrine)
- If a routine is "always done," verify it in person at least once a quarter.
- If you can’t count it, you can’t improve it — find a number, however imperfect.
- If you don’t know why someone isn’t doing the right thing, ask "Why can’t you?" before "Why don’t you?"
- If you’re tempted to complain, write or count instead.
- If you’re uncertain, return to the bedside one more time.
- If you can’t defend the action out loud, don’t take it.

## Layer 3 — Meta (How do we evaluate and improve?)

### Metrics (leading, particulars-tied)
- **Diligence floor adherence** (% of basic rituals completed daily).
- **"One more check" interventions / week** (and what they caught).
- **Things being counted right now** (target: ≥ 1 per role).
- **Things written / month** (notes, memos, post-mortems shared).
- **Unscripted questions / week** (and surprising answers received).
- **Positive deviant behaviors copied / quarter**.

### Review loop
- **Weekly (10–15 min)**:
  - Did the diligence floor hold?
  - What did "one more check" catch?
  - What was counted? What was written?
- **Monthly**: identify one positive deviant in your system; study and copy one behavior.
- **Quarterly**: refresh the basics list — has the floor drifted?

### Core rule guardrail
- If you’re innovating before the basics are reliably done, stop. The work of *better* starts at the floor: hand-washing-tier rituals first, ingenuity second. Without diligence, ingenuity papers over the cracks.
