# Study Guide: Performance and Ethics in Modern Medicine

This study guide provides a comprehensive review of the themes, case studies, and systemic challenges presented in the excerpts from *Better: A Surgeon’s Notes on Performance* by Atul Gawande. It is divided into a short-answer quiz, an answer key, essay prompts for further reflection, and a glossary of key terms.

## Part II: Answer Key

1.  While competence involves mastering the general evolution and treatment of a disease, the senior resident displayed performance by grasping the specific particulars of a patient’s condition in a unique moment. He utilized available resources and exhibited a "tiny act of conscientiousness" by personally checking on a patient despite slow elevators and a busy schedule, ultimately saving her from septic shock.
2.  Semmelweis faced professional pushback because colleagues were offended by the suggestion that doctors were killing their own patients, and they adhered to the "miasma" theory of air-borne disease. Additionally, Semmelweis was a "failed genius" who refused to publish formal evidence and instead chose to viciously attack his detractors, eventually leading to his dismissal.
3.  Positive deviance is the strategy of finding "insiders" who succeed despite having the same resources as their struggling peers. In Vietnam, villagers identified poor mothers whose children were well-nourished because they practiced "deviant" behaviors, such as feeding children with diarrhea or adding sweet potato greens to rice, and then spread those local solutions to the rest of the community.
4.  Smallpox was easily identified by visible blisters, allowing for "ring immunization" of contacts, whereas polio often presents as a silent stomach flu, making it difficult to identify carriers. Furthermore, the oral polio vaccine often requires multiple doses because children with diarrheal illnesses may pass the vaccine through their systems without achieving immunity.
5.  FSTs brought surgical care directly to the battlefield in highly mobile units, reducing the time to treatment during the "Golden Five Minutes" of critical blood loss. By focusing on "damage control" (stabilizing life-threatening injuries rather than performing definitive repairs), these teams significantly contributed to a reduction in battle mortality from 24% to 10%.
6.  In Britain, the General Medical Council requires a chaperone of the appropriate gender to be offered for all "intimate examinations" (breasts, genitalia, or rectum) regardless of the doctor's gender. In the United States, standards are less uniform and often depend on the individual physician's discretion, leading to inconsistencies that can compromise patient trust or leave doctors vulnerable to accusations.
7.  Lang seeks cases that meet two specific requirements: the doctor must have been "negligent" by breaching the duty of care, and that negligence must have caused significant "damage." He often turns away cases where negligence occurred but resulted in no harm, or where the potential jury award would not cover the high cost of legal experts and litigation.
8.  Hsiao's system measures the amount of work involved in a medical task based on time spent, mental effort/judgment, technical skill, and stress. This resulted in a fee schedule that assigns "relative values" to thousands of services, allowing insurers to pay doctors based on the intensity of the work rather than simply paying whatever fees doctors deemed "usual and customary."
9.  The AMA posits that physicians are members of a profession dedicated to preserving life and should not act as instruments of the state in executions. The code of ethics specifies that participation—including monitoring vital signs, giving technical advice, or even being present—is a violation of the physician's role as a healer.
10. At a time when babies born two months premature were considered hopeless, Bowes decided to treat them as if they would live, regardless of how "blue" or "weak" they appeared. His team used existing technologies like respirators and fetal monitors more aggressively, proving that the vast majority of these infants could survive and thrive if doctors fought for them.

## Part IV: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Apgar Score** | A meaningful way to measure the performance and health of a newborn immediately after birth. |
| **Chaperone** | A third person (usually a medical professional) present during an intimate physical examination to ensure propriety and witness the interaction. |
| **Cushing’s Syndrome** | A hormonal disease where the adrenal glands enlarge and produce massive, damaging amounts of the steroid hormone cortisol. |
| **Damage Control** | A surgical strategy used in trauma (specifically by FSTs) that focuses on stopping hemorrhage and controlling contamination rather than definitive repair. |
| **Diligence** | The constant and earnest effort to accomplish what is undertaken; the necessity of giving sufficient attention to detail to avoid error. |
| **Forward Surgical Team (FST)** | A small, 20-person mobile medical unit that operates directly behind troops to provide immediate surgical resuscitation on the battlefield. |
| **Ingenuity** | A requirement for success defined as "thinking anew"; it is a matter of character involving a willingness to recognize failure and search for new solutions. |
| **Mop-up** | A targeted, rapid immunization campaign (usually lasting three days) designed to saturate a population surrounding a newly identified case of a disease. |
| **Negligence** | A legal and professional term defined as a situation where a physician has breached their duty of care to a patient. |
| **Positive Deviance** | The observation and replication of successful behaviors found within a community ("insiders") that differ from the failing norm. |
| **Puerperal Fever** | Also known as "childbed fever," a bacterial infection (usually *Streptococcus*) that was a leading cause of maternal death until hand-washing was mandated. |
| **Relative Value Unit (RVU)** | A calculated value for a medical service based on time, mental effort, technical skill, and stress, used to determine physician compensation. |
| **Septic Shock** | A life-threatening condition resulting from an overwhelming infection (such as fulminant pneumonia) that causes low blood pressure and organ failure. |
| **Shoulder Dystocia** | An obstetric emergency where the baby's head emerges but the shoulders become stuck behind the mother's pubic bone, risking asphyxiation. |
| **VRE / MRSA** | Common shorthand for superresistant bacteria (*Vancomycin-resistant Enterococcus* and *Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus*) that cause hospital-acquired infections. |
