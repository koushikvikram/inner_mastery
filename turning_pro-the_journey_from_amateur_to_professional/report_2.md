# Study Guide: Turning Pro by Steven Pressfield

This study guide provides a comprehensive review of the themes, concepts, and philosophies presented in Steven Pressfield's *Turning Pro*. It explores the transition from the amateur life—characterized by Resistance and shadow careers—to the disciplined, purposeful life of the professional.

## Short-Answer Quiz

1. **How does the text define "Resistance"?**
2. **What is a "shadow career," and why do people pursue them?**
3. **According to the author, what is the fundamental difference between the habits of an amateur and a professional?**
4. **What is the specific "payoff" of addictions and shadow careers?**
5. **How does the author distinguish between a "tramp," a "hobo," and a "bum"?**
6. **Why does the amateur fear "the tribe," and how does this fear affect their work?**
7. **Describe the role of "Art" in Rosanne Cash's "turning pro" dream.**
8. **What is the "10,000 Hour Rule," and what is the key to its success according to the text?**
9. **Explain the Kabbalistic concept of the yetzer hara.**
10. **What does the author mean by the phrase "sit chilly"?**

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

## Answer Key

1. **Resistance:** Resistance is described as a malignant internal entity or a "chattering critic" inside the head that keeps individuals from their true calling. It is the force that stops people from committing to important work and fighting to get it done.
2. **Shadow Career:** A shadow career is a metaphor for a real career; it has a similar shape and feel but entails no real risk of failure. People pursue them because they are terrified of embracing their true calling, allowing them to feel powerful without facing the consequences of failing at what they actually love.
3. **Amateur vs. Professional Habits:** The difference lies in their daily discipline: an amateur has amateur habits (distraction, denial, and fleeing from fear), while a professional has professional habits. A professional shows up every day, stays on the job, and commits to a rigorous, daily exercise of will and focused intention.
4. **Payoff of Addictions:** The primary payoff is "incapacity." By engaging in repetitive, progress-free behaviors like substance abuse or "shadow symphonies," the individual is "off the hook" and excused from the agonizing imperative of identifying and realizing their truest, noblest dreams.
5. **Tramp, Hobo, and Bum:** Based on the explanation from the character Dave, a tramp is an itinerant worker, while a hobo is an itinerant non-worker. A bum is defined as a non-itinerant non-worker.
6. **Fear of the Tribe:** The amateur fears being excluded from the tribe (family, peers, or society) if they reveal who they truly are or what they are capable of. This fear of being seen as "different" or "crazy" leads to paralysis, causing the amateur to seek third-party validation and pander to the expectations of others rather than being authentic.
7. **Art in Rosanne Cash's Dream:** In the dream, "Art" is an elderly man sitting between Cash and Linda Ronstadt who views Cash with disdain. He tells her, "We don't respect dilettantes," which serves as a humiliating epiphany that forces her to commit to her work with the discipline of a true artist.
8. **10,000 Hour Rule:** The rule postulates that achieving mastery in any field requires approximately 10,000 hours of practice. The key is that the practice must be "focused" and possess "intention," aiming to get better and work closer to the bone.
9. **Yetzer Hara:** The yetzer hara is an inner shadow self or a cunning intelligence that operates in opposition to the soul (neshama). Its sole aim is to block the individual from accessing wisdom and goodness, effectively serving as the Kabbalistic equivalent of Resistance.
10. **Sit Chilly:** Originally a term from horsemanship, it means to maintain one's "seat" or composure even when experiencing terror or panic. For the professional, it signifies staying focused on the work and the goal despite the inevitable moments of fear that arise during a pursuit.

## Essay Questions

1. **The Metaphor of the Shadow Life:** Analyze Pressfield's assertion that addictions and shadow careers are "messages in a bottle from our unconscious." How do these behaviors serve as "coded versions" of higher aspirations, and how does recognizing this metaphor facilitate the transition to professional life?
2. **The Anatomy of an Epiphany:** Using the examples of Ms. X, Rosanne Cash, and the author's own experience with the "Smith-Corona," discuss the role of "hitting bottom" in the process of turning pro. Why does the text argue that an epiphany must "trash us" and "strip away self-delusion" to be effective?
3. **The Sacredness of Practice:** Explore the concept of "work as a practice" as described in the text. How do elements like "sacred space," "ritual," and "order" help an artist move beyond the ego and approach the "mystery" or the "Muse"?
4. **Ego vs. Self:** Contrast the amateur's identification with the "ego" with the professional's identification with the "Self." How does the shift from a hierarchical view of the world to a "territorial" or work-centered view change an individual's relationship with their peers and their own output?
5. **The Cost of Professionalism:** Turning pro is described as "free, but not without cost." Discuss the sacrifices mentioned in the text—such as the loss of friends, the end of certain relationships, and the "interior odyssey" of pain—required to survive the passage from amateur to professional.

## Glossary of Key Terms

* **Amateur:** An individual characterized by fear, ego-identification, and a need for third-party validation who lives in denial of their true calling.
* **Resistance:** A malignant internal force and "chattering critic" that creates fear, self-doubt, and self-sabotage to prevent an individual from doing their work.
* **Shadow Career:** A pursuit that serves as a metaphor for one's true calling but involves no real risk and produces no real progress.
* **The Tribe:** The perceived social group or family whose imagined judgment and rejection keep the amateur in a state of paralysis and inauthenticity.
* **Incapacity:** The psychological "payoff" of addiction or self-sabotage; it excuses the individual from the struggle of realizing their destiny.
* **Practice:** A rigorous, daily regimen of commitment and focused intention aimed at mastery and communion with a power greater than oneself.
* **Yetzer Hara:** A Kabbalistic term for the "shadow self" or inner resistance that seeks to block the soul's communication with the individual.
* **Mussar:** A code of ethical discipline in Jewish mysticism used to identify and eliminate "sin" (or Resistance) through humble application of the mind and will.
* **Epiphany:** A moment of intense clarity, often painful, that strips away self-delusion and forces a choice between continuing a "shadow life" or turning pro.
* **The Muse:** The source of inspiration and "unseen powers" that the professional serves through labor and dedication.
* **Self-Abnegation:** The professional's act of setting aside the little-s "self" (ego, distraction, addiction) to serve the work and the higher Self.
* **Pulling the Pin:** A slang term for quitting or uncoupling oneself from a commitment, often used by the author to describe his early failures to finish projects.
* **Sit Chilly:** A directive to remain composed and maintain one's "seat" or focus in the face of panic, terror, or intense Resistance.
* **Conventional Rewards:** External validations such as money, applause, and fame, which the text distinguishes from the "psychological salary" of the work itself.
* **Shamanism:** As used in the text, the practice of accessing unseen powers (both good and evil) to navigate the spiritual aspects of the creative struggle.
