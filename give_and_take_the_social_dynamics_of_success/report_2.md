# Study Guide: Give and Take by Adam Grant

It is designed to assist in mastering the concepts of reciprocity styles, the dynamics of success, and the impact of interpersonal choices in professional environments.

## Part 2: Answer Key

1. **Reciprocity Styles:** Takers strive to get more than they give, tilting reciprocity in their own favor to protect their interests. Givers are other-focused, preferring to contribute more than they receive by sharing time, energy, and knowledge. Matchers operate on the principle of fairness and "tit for tat," striving to maintain an equal balance of giving and getting.
2. **Givers' Success Polarization:** At the bottom, givers may sacrifice their own productivity and become "chumps" by being too trusting or caring. However, at the top, they become "champs" by building goodwill and reputations that create a ripple effect of value for everyone around them, eventually leading to sustainable success.
3. **Lekking:** In the CEO world, lekking is a self-glorifying display where takers signal their perceived superiority, often through large, full-page photos of themselves in annual reports. Other signs include a high frequency of first-person singular pronouns (I, me, mine) in interviews and demanding pay gaps where they earn significantly more than other executives.
4. **Belgian Medical Students:** In their first year, givers had lower grades because they spent time helping peers study instead of focusing on their own knowledge gaps. However, as they transitioned into clinical rotations and patient care in later years, their natural tendencies toward teamwork and service allowed them to substantially outperform their peers.
5. **Dormant Ties:** Dormant ties are people with whom you have lost touch but who still maintain a foundation of mutual trust. They are highly valuable because they provide novel information and "fresh ideas" gathered from different social circles, which current contacts (who share your same information pool) cannot offer.
6. **The Five-Minute Favor:** This is the rule that one should be willing to do a small favor taking five minutes or less for anyone without expecting anything in return. Adam Rifkin uses this to add value to his network, sparking a contagious form of reciprocity where people feel motivated to pay help forward to others.
7. **Responsibility Bias:** This is the tendency of individuals to exaggerate their own contributions to a project relative to others because they have more access to information about their own efforts. It can destroy collaborations when participants feel they aren't getting due credit, though givers mitigate this by focusing on what others have contributed.
8. **Expedition Behavior:** It involves being selfless and putting the group’s mission and goals ahead of personal interests, as seen in NASA shuttle crews and mountain climbers. George Meyer exemplified this on *The Simpsons* by taking on "dirty work" like rewrites and helping others with their jokes to make the collective show better.
9. **Self-Fulfilling Prophecy:** This occurs when a leader’s high expectations for a subordinate's potential lead to supportive behaviors that actually boost that person’s confidence and performance. Studies by Dov Eden and Robert Rosenthal showed that when teachers or commanders were told randomly selected individuals were "bloomers," those individuals actually showed greater gains.
10. **Escalation of Commitment:** This is the tendency to invest more resources into a failing course of action to protect one’s ego and save face. Takers are more susceptible because they view failure as a personal threat and discount negative feedback, whereas givers are more willing to admit mistakes to protect the organization.

## Part 4: Glossary of Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Giver** | An individual who prefers to give more than they get, focusing on what others need and helping without expecting anything in return. |
| **Taker** | An individual who aims to get more than they give, putting their own interests ahead of others' needs and viewing the world as competitive. |
| **Matcher** | An individual who operates on the principle of fairness, seeking an equal balance of giving and getting through "tit for tat" reciprocity. |
| **Lekking** | A ritual of showing off; in business, it refers to taker CEOs using self-glorifying displays like large photos in annual reports to signal status. |
| **Dormant Ties** | Relationships with former colleagues or acquaintances that have not been active for years but still contain a baseline of trust. |
| **Five-Minute Favor** | A commitment to performing small, low-cost acts of kindness for others without seeking a specific personal return. |
| **Responsibility Bias** | The psychological tendency to overestimate one's own contributions to a collective task while underestimating the contributions of others. |
| **Expedition Behavior** | A selfless approach to teamwork that prioritizes the group's mission and the well-being of others over personal ego. |
| **Idiosyncrasy Credits** | Positive "points" or impressions earned through giving that grant a person the license to deviate from group norms or challenge the status quo. |
| **Psychological Safety** | The belief that one can take risks or make mistakes in a group setting without being penalized or punished. |
| **Perspective Gap** | The inability to accurately imagine another person's intense psychological or physical state (like pain or stress) when one is not currently experiencing it. |
| **Grit** | A combination of passion and perseverance toward long-term goals, often more predictive of success than raw intelligence. |
| **Escalation of Commitment** | Continuing to invest in a losing project or "bad bet" primarily to justify a previous decision and protect one's ego. |
| **Sunk Cost Fallacy** | A bias where individuals take past, unrecoverable investments into account when making decisions about future spending. |
| **Powerless Communication** | A style of interaction characterized by modesty, vulnerability, and asking questions rather than asserting dominance. |
