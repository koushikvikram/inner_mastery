# Epictetus Operating Model — Sita's Counsel to Rama, *The Subtle Shades of Dharma*


![Epictetus Operating Model — Sita's Counsel to Rama, *The Subtle Shades of Dharma*](./epictetus_operating_model.png)

> Source frame: in the Dandaka forest, **Sita** challenges **Rama** on the **subtle shades of dharma** — righteousness is not a fixed rulebook but a path "difficult to discern," knowable cleanly only by one without desire. She names **three sins to avoid**, ranked by severity: (1) **falsehood**, (2) **coveting another's wife**, (3) — worst — **hurting one who has not offended you (unprovoked violence)**. Her critique is the **paradox of the ascetic-warrior**: while wearing the *tapasvi*'s garb you should live by ascetic rules, and the bow is "firewood to fire" — for a *kshatriya*, the mere touch of the weapon breeds the itch to kill, and "to think is to act." Rama answers from a different hierarchy: the **sanctity of the given word** and the **duty to protect the vulnerable** *rishis* — "I may give up my life, Lakshmana, or Sita, but I must keep my promise." The model holds both poles: dharma is **context-dependent and role-bound**, weighed against the three sins, governed by promise-keeping, and disciplined by awareness that **the tools we carry shape the deeds we do**.

## Layer 1 — Practice (What must be done?)

### What we’re trying to achieve
- Discern the **right action in *this* context**, not a generic rule — honoring the "subtle shades" of dharma.
- Avoid the **three sins** absolutely, especially the gravest: harming the unoffending.
- Keep the **given word inviolable**, ranking promise-keeping above comfort and even safety.
- Match conduct to the **role I am actually in** (ascetic vs. warrior), and notice how my **tools** bias me toward action.

### Non-negotiable behaviors
- **Run the three-sins test** on any consequential act: Is there falsehood? Coveting another's spouse? Harm to one who has not offended me?
- **Protect the vulnerable who seek refuge** — duty to those under your protection is foundational *kshatriya dharma*.
- **Keep every promise once given** — the word outranks convenience, and even personal stakes.
- **Read your role before acting**: live by the rules of the garb you wear right now.
- **Audit the weapon**: notice that carrying a tool ("firewood") inclines you to use it; account for that pull.

### Routines / checklists

- **Three-Sins gate (before any forceful or consequential act)**
  - **Falsehood?** — Is anything I'm about to say or imply untrue?
  - **Coveting?** — Am I reaching for what belongs to another?
  - **Unprovoked harm?** — Has the target actually offended/threatened, or am I striking the innocent? (worst of the three — stop here if unclear.)

- **Role-and-garb check (context discernment)**
  - Name the role I'm in *now* (ascetic, protector, householder, ruler).
  - Ask: what does *this* role permit and forbid in *this* setting?
  - Reserve other-role duties for their proper time and place (kshatriya dharma for Ayodhya, not the hermitage).

- **The weapon audit (tool-awareness)**
  - List the "bows" I'm carrying — capacities that create an "itch to act" (power, authority, a sharp tongue, a weapon, leverage).
  - Ask: is this tool pulling me toward action the situation doesn't truly require?
  - Decide deliberately whether to carry it here at all.

- **Promise ledger (the inviolable word)**
  - Before giving a word: can I keep it across all foreseeable costs? If not, don't pledge.
  - Once given: rank it above comfort and convenience; "I may give up much, but I keep my promise."

- **Provocation vs. protection weighing (Rama–Sita test)**
  - Is my action *retaliation against the unoffending* (Sita's warning) or *protection of those who sought refuge* (Rama's duty)?
  - Where the suffering of the vulnerable constitutes a real call, protection can justify force; absent that, restraint governs.

### What good execution looks like
- Every consequential act clears the three-sins gate, with the gravest sin (unprovoked harm) treated as a hard stop.
- Decisions visibly fit the role and context, not a one-size rule.
- Promises are rare, deliberate, and then kept regardless of cost.
- You can distinguish *protecting the vulnerable* from *striking the unoffending* — and act accordingly.
- You notice when a tool is manufacturing the urge to use it, and you carry it (or not) on purpose.

## Layer 2 — Principles (Why does it work?)

- **Dharma has subtle shades**: righteousness is context-dependent; the same act can be duty in one role and sin in another.
- **Desire clouds discernment**: only one without desire follows dharma "without swerving a hair's breadth"; desire biases the reading.
- **Hierarchy of sins**: falsehood < coveting another's wife < harming the unoffending — and the gravest must never be rationalized.
- **The word is sacred**: a promise once given outranks comfort, possessions, and even life — it is identity, not preference.
- **Duty to the vulnerable**: protecting those who seek refuge is a primary obligation of the one with power to protect.
- **Tools shape conduct**: "the bow is firewood to fire" — what you carry reshapes what you're inclined to do; for the decisive, to think is to act.
- **Role determines duty**: the garb you wear should govern your conduct in the moment.

### Decision rules (doctrine)
- If an act would harm someone who has not offended → stop; this is the gravest sin, not justified by zeal.
- If a deed involves falsehood or coveting → it fails the gate regardless of benefit.
- If you've given your word → keep it, even at great personal cost; if you can't keep it, don't give it.
- If the vulnerable under your protection are being harmed → protective force can be dharma even absent personal offense.
- If a tool is generating the urge to act → decide consciously whether the situation, not the tool, calls for it.
- If your role and your impulse conflict → let the role you're actually in govern.

## Layer 3 — Meta (How do we evaluate and improve?)

### Metrics (leading, conduct-tied)
- **Three-sins gate runs / week** (consequential acts screened before action).
- **Promises made vs. kept** (target: few made, all kept).
- **Role-context matches** (decisions that fit the garb/role you were actually in).
- **Unprovoked-harm near-misses caught** (impulses to strike the unoffending, intercepted).
- **Weapon audits** (instances where you noticed a tool biasing you toward unnecessary action).

### Review loop
- **Daily**: Did any action risk the third sin? Which role was I in, and did my conduct fit it? Did a tool pull me toward acting?
- **Weekly**: Did desire distort any reading of duty? Were promises given carefully and kept?
- **Monthly**: Across hard cases, can I cleanly separate *protecting the vulnerable* from *harming the unoffending* — and is my discernment of the subtle shades sharpening?

### Core rule guardrail
- Never let a noble aim or a sacred vow become a license for the gravest sin — **harming one who has not offended you**. Sita's warning stands over Rama's resolve: keep the word and protect the refuge-seeker, but the moment "duty" starts justifying violence against the innocent, the bow has become firewood and you are following the itch, not the dharma. Pause, re-run the three-sins gate, and re-read your role.

## Operating Sequence

1. **Discern the context**: name your role and garb; recall that dharma's shades are subtle and desire distorts them.
2. **Run the three-sins gate**: screen for falsehood, coveting, and — the hard stop — unprovoked harm.
3. **Weigh provocation vs. protection**: distinguish striking the unoffending from protecting those who sought refuge.
4. **Audit the weapon**: notice which tools are manufacturing the urge to act; carry them deliberately or not at all.
5. **Honor the word**: pledge rarely, then keep the promise above comfort and cost.
6. **Review by the gravest sin**: each evening, confirm no action crossed into harming the innocent, and refine your reading of the subtle shades.
